<h2 id="interview-framing">Interview Framing</h2>

A web crawler systematically downloads the web. Starting from a set of **seed URLs**, it fetches each page, extracts the links it contains, and follows them — fanning out across the web graph to build a corpus of pages for a search index, an archive, or a dataset. Googlebot, Bingbot, the Internet Archive's crawler, and the bots behind every search engine are this system. From the outside it's the textbook graph traversal: a queue of URLs, a loop that pops one, downloads it, parses out links, and pushes the new ones back. That framing produces a crawler that works on a thousand pages and then becomes a menace at a billion — hammering servers it shouldn't, re-downloading pages it already has, drowning in a queue it can't fit in memory, and getting trapped in infinite link mazes.

The hard part is doing this **at web scale, politely, without redoing work**. Three problems dominate, and none of them is the downloading. First, the **URL frontier** — the queue of what to crawl next — is the whole design: it can't be a simple FIFO, because it must enforce **politeness** (never more than one in-flight request to a host, with a delay between them, so the crawler doesn't look like a denial-of-service attack) *and* **priority** (crawl important and fresh pages before obscure ones), across a distributed fleet of fetchers. Second, **deduplication at scale**: most links you extract point to pages you've already seen, so you need a **seen-URL set** of tens of billions of entries — and storing that exactly is terabytes, so it has to be a probabilistic **bloom filter**; separately, the same page is reachable by many URLs, so you also dedup **content**. Third, **robustness and freshness**: the web is hostile (infinite calendars, session-id explosions, malformed pages — **crawler traps**) and it changes, so you must bound traps and **re-crawl** on a freshness schedule. The reframe to lead with: *it looks like BFS with a queue, but the queue is the entire problem — a priority-and-politeness URL frontier across a distributed fleet, a seen-set too large to store exactly so it's a bloom filter, content dedup so the same page isn't stored twice, and trap-survival plus freshness re-crawling. Fetching a page is trivial; choosing which URL to fetch, when, politely, without repeating yourself, at web scale, is the design.*

Spend the opening minutes scoping. Good questions to ask:

- What's the crawl for — a search index, archiving, a research dataset? It shapes what you store and how fresh it must be.
- How big and how fresh — one pass of N billion pages, or continuous re-crawling? Continuous adds the freshness/scheduling dimension.
- Must we render JavaScript, or is fetching raw HTML enough? Headless rendering is far more expensive and is often a separate tier.
- How polite must we be — strict robots.txt and per-host limits? (Almost always yes — politeness is non-negotiable.)
- Single data center or globally distributed? Scale drives how the frontier and seen-set are sharded.

Three targets shape every later decision. First, **politeness is a hard constraint, not a nicety**: a crawler that ignores robots.txt or floods a server is indistinguishable from an attack and will get blocked (and is unethical), so politeness — one request per host at a time, a crawl delay, obeying robots — is built into the **structure** of the frontier, not bolted on. Second, **dedup must be space-efficient**: at tens of billions of URLs the seen-set can't be an exact in-memory hash table (terabytes), so it's a **bloom filter**, trading rare false positives for a ~40× space saving — without this the crawler simply can't run. Third, **freshness with bounded work**: the web changes constantly but you can't re-crawl everything equally often, so re-crawl frequency is **tuned to each page's change rate** and importance. Note what's *relaxed*: coverage is **best-effort** (you'll never crawl "all" of an effectively infinite web), and the corpus is **eventually consistent** with the live web (a page crawled yesterday is fine). The system trades completeness and instant freshness for politeness, scale, and bounded, prioritized work.

The takeaways. First, **fetching is ordinary throughput**: crawling 30 billion pages in 30 days is ~**11,574 pages/sec**, needing ~**116 fetcher nodes** and ~**9.26 Gbps** of sustained bandwidth — large, but a straightforward matter of parallel async I/O and enough network. Second — the signature lesson — **the seen-set is the real problem**: to avoid re-crawling, you must remember every URL you've encountered, and at 30 billion URLs an *exact* set costs ~**1.5 TB** (too big to hold in RAM on a node), while a **bloom filter** holds the same set in ~**37.5 GB** — a ~**40× reduction** — at the cost of rare false positives, where a hash collision makes you wrongly think you've seen a page and skip it. That trade (occasionally missing a page versus needing terabytes of RAM) is overwhelmingly worth it, and it's *the* reason crawlers use bloom filters. Third, **the corpus is multi-petabyte**: ~**3 PB** of page content, which lives in a distributed blob store, not the crawler's memory. The capacity story: the crawler is hard because of the **frontier and the seen-set** — managing and deduplicating an enormous URL space — not because downloading a page is difficult.

Three supporting entities complete the picture. The discipline: dedup URLs by hash in a bloom filter, partition by host so politeness is local, and fingerprint content so the same page isn't stored twice.

<h2 id="high-level-architecture">High-Level Architecture</h2>

The architecture is a **loop**: the frontier hands a URL to a fetcher, which downloads the page and stores it, a parser extracts links, dedup filters out ones already seen, and the survivors go back into the frontier — with a scheduler re-injecting pages for freshness.

<WebCrawlerArchitecture />

- **Seeds.** The starting URLs that bootstrap the traversal.
- **URL Frontier.** The heart: a priority-and-politeness queue that decides what to crawl next, handing out only URLs whose host is currently free to be crawled. Sharded by host across the fleet.
- **Fetcher.** Downloads pages — resolves DNS (cached), checks robots.txt, makes one polite request per host, handles timeouts and redirects. Massively concurrent async I/O.
- **DNS resolver.** Resolves hostnames to IPs, **cached** aggressively because a lookup per host is otherwise a bottleneck.
- **Robots cache.** Per-host `robots.txt` rules and crawl-delay, cached with a TTL.
- **Parser.** Extracts and normalizes links from fetched pages.
- **Seen-Set.** The bloom-filter dedup structure — checks whether a discovered URL has been encountered before, so the crawler doesn't loop forever.
- **Content Store.** The petabyte-scale blob store of fetched pages, with content fingerprints for dedup.
- **Scheduler.** Re-enqueues pages for re-crawl on a freshness cadence.

The decisive boundary is **the frontier governs what to crawl and when (priority + politeness), and the seen-set governs whether to crawl it at all (dedup)** — everything else is the mechanical fetch-parse-store loop around them. The frontier's per-host structure makes politeness automatic; the bloom-filter seen-set makes dedup fit in memory; DNS and robots caches keep the per-fetch overhead low; and the scheduler closes the loop for freshness. Every section that follows elaborates one piece: how the frontier balances priority and politeness, how politeness/robots work, how dedup stays space-efficient, how DNS and fetching scale, how freshness is scheduled, and how the crawler survives the hostile web.

<h2 id="detailed-flows">Detailed Flows</h2>

Four flows cover the system: the core fetch-and-extract loop, the dedup check on a discovered URL, the politeness check before hitting a host, and the freshness re-crawl. The first is the main loop; the others are the guards and the refresh that keep it correct and current.

**Fetch a page.** The frontier hands the fetcher the next URL; the fetcher downloads the page, stores it in the content store, and the parser extracts its links — which then head toward the dedup check before any re-enter the frontier. This is the loop that traverses the web.

<FetchPageSequence />

**Dedup a discovered URL.** Each extracted link is checked against the **bloom-filter seen-set**. If it's already seen, it's dropped (the common case — most links are re-discoveries). If it's new, it's added to the set and enqueued in the frontier. This is what keeps the crawler from looping forever over the same pages.

<DedupCheckSequence />

**Apply politeness.** Before contacting a host, the fetcher loads its cached `robots.txt`, confirms the URL is allowed and the per-host crawl delay has elapsed, and only then makes a single request. Disallowed or too-soon URLs are skipped or deferred — the crawler stays a good citizen.

<PolitenessSequence />

**Re-crawl for freshness.** The scheduler reads a page's last-crawled time and its estimated change rate, decides it's due for a refresh, and re-enqueues it into the frontier — fast-changing pages (news) come back often, static ones rarely.

<h2 id="url-frontier">The URL Frontier</h2>

The frontier is the centerpiece, and the key insight is that **a plain FIFO queue is wrong** on two counts: it has no notion of **priority** (it would crawl a trivial page before an important one just because it was discovered first) and no notion of **politeness** (it would hand a fetcher ten URLs for the same host back-to-back, hammering it). The classic solution is the **Mercator two-level design**. A set of **front queues**, one per priority level, handles prioritization: a URL is routed to a front queue by its importance (PageRank-ish signals, freshness, depth), and the selector pulls from higher-priority front queues more often. A set of **back queues**, each dedicated to a **single host**, handles politeness: every URL for a host goes to that host's back queue, and the rule is that only one URL per back queue (per host) is in flight at a time, with a **heap** tracking the earliest time each host may next be contacted (its last-fetch time plus its crawl delay). A fetcher asks the heap for the next host whose time has come, takes one URL from that host's back queue, and crawls it — so politeness is **structural**, a consequence of the queue layout, not a sleep statement scattered in the fetcher.

At scale the frontier is **sharded across the fleet by host**: a given host's URLs (and thus its politeness state) live on one shard, so no cross-node coordination is needed to avoid two fetchers hitting the same server simultaneously. The frontier is also **huge and persistent** — hundreds of millions to billions of pending URLs — so it's backed by disk/distributed storage with in-memory front/back queue heads, not held entirely in RAM. The principle: the frontier reconciles two competing goals — crawl the *right* pages (priority) and crawl *nicely* (politeness) — with a two-level queue where front queues encode priority and per-host back queues encode politeness, sharded by host so the politeness invariant holds without coordination.

<h2 id="politeness">Politeness & robots.txt</h2>

Politeness is what separates a legitimate crawler from an attack, and it's non-negotiable. The first rule is **robots.txt**: before crawling a host, the crawler fetches `/robots.txt`, parses which paths are **disallowed** for its user-agent, and honors any **`Crawl-delay`** directive — then **caches** those rules per host (with a TTL) so it isn't re-fetched every request. URLs that robots disallows are never crawled. The second rule is **rate limiting per host**: at most one in-flight request to a host at a time and a minimum delay between requests (a default like a few seconds, or the host's stated crawl-delay), so a small site isn't overwhelmed by the crawler's firepower. The third is **identification**: a descriptive `User-Agent` with a contact URL, so site owners know who's crawling and can reach you — and so you can be a good web citizen.

The important design point is that politeness is enforced **structurally**, by the frontier's per-host back queues (previous section), not by hopeful `sleep()` calls in the fetcher. Because all of a host's URLs live in one back queue on one shard, and the heap won't release that host until its delay has elapsed, the *only* way the system can issue a request to a host is the polite way — there's no path that bypasses it. This matters because a distributed fleet makes ad-hoc politeness fragile: two fetchers that independently decide "I'll wait two seconds" can still hit the same server at the same instant. Centralizing a host's scheduling in one place removes that race. The principle: obey robots.txt (cached), cap to one request per host with a crawl delay, identify yourself — and make all of that a property of where URLs live, so politeness can't be accidentally violated by a distributed fleet.

<h2 id="dedup">URL & Content Deduplication</h2>

There are **two** dedup problems, and conflating them is a common mistake. The first is **URL dedup**: as you extract links, the overwhelming majority point to URLs you've already discovered, so you need a **seen-set** to answer "have I encountered this URL before?" — otherwise the crawl loops forever. At tens of billions of URLs, an exact set (a hash table of the URL strings or their hashes) is **terabytes** and won't fit in a node's memory, so the standard structure is a **bloom filter**: a bit array with several hash functions that answers set-membership in ~10 bits per URL (the capacity model's 37.5 GB versus 1.5 TB). The cost is **false positives** — occasionally the filter says "seen" for a URL you haven't actually crawled, so you skip a page you should have fetched — but never false negatives, so you never re-crawl something you've already done. For a crawler, rarely missing a page is a perfectly acceptable price for fitting the seen-set in memory.

The second is **content dedup**: many distinct URLs serve the **same or nearly the same content** (mirror sites, print/mobile variants, session-id URLs, syndicated articles). Crawling and storing each separately wastes bandwidth, storage, and index space. **Exact** duplicates are caught with a **content hash** (e.g. an MD5/SHA of the normalized body): identical bytes, identical hash, store once. **Near**-duplicates — pages that differ only in a timestamp, an ad, or boilerplate — are caught with a **similarity fingerprint** like **simhash** or **minhash**, where similar documents produce similar fingerprints, so you can detect "this is 98% the same as a page I already have" and skip it. The principle: dedup URLs with a space-efficient bloom filter (accepting rare false positives to fit in memory) so the traversal terminates, and dedup content with exact hashes plus near-duplicate fingerprints so the same page isn't stored and processed many times.

<h2 id="dns-fetching">DNS Resolution & Fetching</h2>

Two parts of the fetch path are surprisingly load-bearing. The first is **DNS**: every host you crawl must be resolved to an IP, and a naive crawler that does a synchronous DNS lookup per URL discovers that DNS — not bandwidth — is its bottleneck, because resolution can take tens to hundreds of milliseconds and public resolvers rate-limit. The fixes are to **cache** DNS results aggressively (hosts are hit many times, and records have TTLs) and to resolve **asynchronously**, often with a dedicated local resolver or a pre-resolved cache, so DNS never blocks the fetchers. The second is **fetching** itself: downloading at ~11,000+ pages/sec means **thousands of concurrent connections**, which only works with **non-blocking async I/O** (an event loop or async runtime), not a thread per request. The fetcher must also handle the messy realities of HTTP — **timeouts** (a slow server can't be allowed to tie up a worker), **redirects** (followed up to a limit), **content-type and size limits** (don't download a 2 GB file or a video when you want HTML), and compression.

These details are where a crawler's real throughput is won or lost. A crawler is fundamentally an **I/O-bound** system: each fetcher spends almost all its time waiting on the network, so the design maximizes concurrency (many simultaneous in-flight requests per node) rather than CPU work. That's why the fetch fleet is sized by how many concurrent connections each node sustains, and why DNS caching matters so much — an un-cached DNS lookup injects a synchronous stall into an otherwise asynchronous pipeline. The principle: treat DNS as a first-class, cached, asynchronous concern rather than an afterthought, and build fetching as massively concurrent non-blocking I/O with strict timeouts and size limits, because the crawler's speed comes from overlapping thousands of slow network waits, not from doing any one fetch quickly.

<h2 id="freshness-scheduling">Freshness & Re-crawl Scheduling</h2>

A one-time crawl is stale the moment it finishes — the web changes constantly — so a real crawler **re-crawls**, and the question is *how often* to revisit each page. You can't crawl everything equally frequently (that's a fixed budget spread infinitely thin), so re-crawl frequency is **tuned per page to its change rate and importance**. A news homepage changes minute-to-minute and is important, so it's re-crawled very often; an archived document hasn't changed in years and is re-crawled rarely. The crawler **estimates each page's change rate** from history — comparing successive fetches (did the content hash change?) to learn how volatile a page is — and schedules `next_crawl` accordingly, often weighting by importance so high-value pages get more freshness budget. This is exactly a time-triggered re-enqueue, the same shape as the **Distributed Job Scheduler**: a scheduler holds pages by their `next_crawl` time and re-injects them into the frontier when due.

The trade-off is **freshness versus coverage** under a fixed crawl budget: every re-crawl of a known page is a fetch you're *not* spending discovering a new one. Crawl too aggressively for freshness and you stop growing the corpus; crawl too little and your index goes stale. Production crawlers tune this balance continuously, and it's a great thing to name in an interview — the budget is finite, so freshness and coverage are in direct competition, and the scheduler's job is to spend each fetch where it has the most value (a likely-changed important page, or a promising new one). The principle: re-crawl on a per-page cadence learned from each page's observed change rate and weighted by importance, implemented as a time-triggered re-enqueue into the frontier, while consciously balancing the finite budget between refreshing known pages and discovering new ones.

<h2 id="traps-robustness">Crawler Traps & Robustness</h2>

The web is full of hostile and degenerate structures, and a naive crawler walks straight into them. **Crawler traps** are URL spaces that are effectively infinite: a calendar with "next month" links forever, faceted-search or filter combinations that explode into billions of URLs, session-ids or tracking parameters that mint a "new" URL on every visit, or deliberately generated link mazes. A crawler that follows links blindly will pour its entire budget into one trap and never escape. The defenses are **bounds**: a **maximum crawl depth** (hops from a seed), **per-host URL budgets** (don't take more than N pages from one site), **URL-pattern detection** (spot and cap parameter-explosion patterns; strip known tracking/session params during normalization), and limiting the number of URLs queued per host. Many traps are also defused for free by **URL normalization** (canonicalizing so `?utm_source=...` variants collapse to one URL) and by content dedup (a trap often serves near-identical pages, which the fingerprint catches).

Beyond traps, **robustness** means surviving the ordinary hostility of real pages: **malformed HTML** (parse defensively, never crash a fetcher on bad markup), **huge pages** (size limits so a fetcher isn't tarpitted by a multi-gigabyte response), **soft-404s** (pages that return 200 but are really "not found" — detected by content patterns), slow servers (timeouts), and transient failures (retry with backoff, then give up and mark failed). The principle: assume the web is adversarial — bound the traversal with depth limits, per-host budgets, and pattern caps so no trap can consume the crawl; normalize URLs and dedup content to collapse degenerate spaces; and parse and fetch defensively so no single bad page can take down a fetcher. A crawler's robustness is mostly the discipline of never trusting what's on the other end of a link.

Don't build the globally-distributed, sharded crawler on day one. Each stage answers a specific pressure and adds a specific cost — name the trigger before taking on the complexity.

The progression tracks the wall you're hitting. A **single-threaded** crawler is I/O-bound and serial — fine for a demo, useless at scale. **Multi-threading on one box** overlaps the network waits but caps out on a single machine's connections and can't hold a multi-terabyte seen-set. The center of gravity for a real crawler is **distributed fetchers with a host-sharded frontier and a bloom-filter seen-set**, plus a distributed content store — this is where politeness holds across the fleet (host-sharding), dedup fits in memory (bloom filter), and throughput scales horizontally. Only continuous web-scale operations need **global distribution with tiered storage**, which pays for geographic reach and a petabyte corpus with cross-region coordination and storage tiering. Most crawlers are well-served by "distributed fetchers, host-sharded priority/politeness frontier, bloom-filter seen-set, distributed content store."

The theme: **lease the work, replicate the state, and bound everything.** A dead fetcher is harmless because URLs are *leased* from the frontier, so an expired lease just returns them to be re-handed out (an at-least-once crawl, where the bloom filter and content dedup absorb the occasional repeat). The frontier and seen-set are **replicated and rebuildable** — the bloom filter can be reconstructed from the crawl log if a shard is lost. Politeness can't be violated because it's structural (per-host back queues), and traps can't drain the crawl because depth, budget, and pattern caps **bound** every host. Bad pages are contained by timeouts and size limits, and content-store hiccups are ridden out by buffering while the frontier holds the URL. Because the frontier is persistent and the seen-set is rebuildable, recovery is almost always "re-lease the URLs and restore the set" — and the crawler's three cardinal sins (impoliteness, infinite re-crawling, trap capture) are each designed out structurally rather than patched reactively.

<h2 id="tradeoffs-alternatives">Trade-offs & Alternatives</h2>

Every recommendation here bought something and gave up something. Stating both is the difference between an answer and a strong answer.

<DecisionRecord
  title="The one-sentence design"
  choice="Traverse the web from seeds with a host-sharded URL frontier that balances priority (front queues) and politeness (per-host back queues with a crawl-delay heap, so only one request per host is in flight); dedup URLs with a bloom-filter seen-set (accepting rare false positives to fit tens of billions of URLs in memory) and content with exact hashes plus simhash near-duplicate fingerprints; fetch with cached async DNS and massively concurrent non-blocking I/O under timeouts and size limits; re-crawl on a per-page freshness schedule; and bound traps with depth limits, per-host budgets, and URL-pattern caps."
  rationale="The hard parts are the frontier and dedup, not fetching. A two-level frontier makes politeness structural (impossible to violate) while still prioritizing important pages, and host-sharding keeps the politeness invariant without coordination. The seen-set must fit in memory at web scale, which only a probabilistic structure achieves, so a bloom filter's rare false positives are a worthwhile trade. DNS caching and async I/O turn fetching into an overlap-the-waits throughput problem. Freshness scheduling spends a finite budget where it's most valuable, and trap bounding keeps any single hostile space from consuming the crawl."
  alternatives="A FIFO frontier — simple but no priority and no politeness (it would hammer hosts and crawl junk first); an exact seen-set (hash table) — precise but terabytes, won't fit in memory; per-fetcher politeness sleeps — fragile in a distributed fleet (independent fetchers collide on a host); synchronous DNS per URL — makes DNS the bottleneck; crawl everything at one frequency — wastes the budget versus change-rate-tuned re-crawling; unbounded link-following — guarantees capture by the first calendar/parameter trap; storing every URL's content — wasteful versus content dedup."
  revisitWhen="JavaScript-heavy sites require headless rendering (a far more expensive fetch tier), the crawl must be globally distributed for locality, the corpus needs tiered/cold storage as it grows to many petabytes, or focused/topical crawling changes prioritization from breadth to a relevance model."
/>

Four axes capture the real tension:

- **Priority crawling vs plain BFS.** BFS is simple but crawls by discovery order; a priority frontier crawls important/fresh pages first, at the cost of computing priorities and a more complex frontier. At scale, priority wins.
- **Exact seen-set vs bloom filter.** Exact never skips a page but costs terabytes; a bloom filter fits in memory with rare false positives (skipped pages). At web scale, the bloom filter is the only option.
- **Politeness vs throughput.** Strict per-host limits cap how fast you can crawl any one site, reducing raw throughput, but they're mandatory — impoliteness gets you blocked and is unethical. You scale throughput by crawling *many* hosts in parallel, not any one host faster.
- **Freshness vs coverage.** A finite budget must split between re-crawling known pages (freshness) and discovering new ones (coverage); the right balance depends on the crawl's purpose and is tuned continuously.
