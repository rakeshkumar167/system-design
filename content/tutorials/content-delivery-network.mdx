<h2 id="interview-framing">Interview Framing</h2>

A content delivery network — Cloudflare, Akamai, Fastly, CloudFront — is the layer that makes the web fast everywhere by serving content from **edge locations physically close to each user** instead of from one distant origin. Several tutorials in this curriculum have already leaned on "the CDN" as a black box: [Pastebin](/learn/pastebin) and [Photo Sharing](/learn/photo-sharing) offload immutable blobs to it, [Video Streaming](/learn/video-streaming) serves ~95% of its segments from it, and [Maps & Navigation](/learn/maps-navigation) delivers map tiles through it. This tutorial **builds that box.** And the apparent job — "copy files onto servers near people" — hides where the real engineering is: a CDN is a **globally distributed cache**, and every hard problem is a distributed-cache problem at planetary scale.

Three forces dominate. First, **request routing**: with hundreds of points of presence (PoPs) worldwide, the system must steer *each* user to the *right* nearby, healthy edge — done at the network layer with **Anycast** (announce one IP from every PoP and let BGP pick the closest) or at the naming layer with **DNS-based geo-routing** (GeoDNS returns the nearest edge's address). Second, **origin offload**: the entire economic and architectural point is to answer as many requests as possible *at the edge* so the origin barely works — which means driving the **cache-hit ratio** as high as possible *and* protecting a single origin from the **thundering herd** of misses that a global fleet can generate (cache hierarchy, origin shield, request coalescing). Third, **cache invalidation**: the same object is cached across thousands of independent edges, so when it changes at the origin, keeping every copy correct is genuinely hard — the famous "only two hard things in computer science" problem — solved with TTLs, active purges, or versioned immutable URLs. The reframe to lead with: *a CDN isn't "files on servers near users" — it's operating a globally distributed cache: route each user to the nearest healthy edge, maximize the cache-hit ratio while shielding a single origin from herds of misses, and keep thousands of cached copies correct when content changes.*

Spend the opening minutes scoping. Good questions to ask:

- What **content** are we serving — mostly static and cacheable (images, video, JS/CSS, downloads), or also dynamic/personalized responses? This sets how much the cache can even do.
- What matters more, **latency** (time-to-first-byte near the user) or **origin offload** (protecting a fragile backend)? Usually both, but the emphasis shapes the design.
- How **fresh** must content be — can we tolerate seconds-to-minutes of staleness (TTLs), or do changes need to reflect near-instantly (active purge)?
- What's the **scale** — request rate and egress bandwidth — and how many edge locations? This reveals the offload math.
- Do we also want the CDN to be a **security perimeter** (TLS, DDoS absorption, WAF)? A CDN naturally doubles as one.

This tutorial commits to concrete assumptions so the design stays consistent end to end. **In scope:** request routing (Anycast/GeoDNS), the edge cache (keys, TTLs, eviction, hit ratio), origin offload (cache hierarchy, origin shield, request coalescing), cache invalidation and freshness, dynamic-content acceleration and edge compute, and edge security — plus scaling, failure modes, and trade-offs. **Out of scope (reuse, don't rebuild):** the *content* being served originates from systems like object storage or the origins in [Pastebin](/learn/pastebin), [Photo Sharing](/learn/photo-sharing), and [Video Streaming](/learn/video-streaming) (we cache and deliver, we don't produce it); per-endpoint eviction/hot-key mechanics are generalized in the [Distributed Cache](/learn/distributed-cache); edge rate limiting reuses the [Rate Limiter](/learn/rate-limiter). Also out of scope: the physical build-out of PoPs and peering, video-specific transcoding, and DNS as its own product. This tutorial composes routing, a caching edge, an origin-protection hierarchy, and an invalidation control plane into one global delivery system.

Three targets shape every later decision. First, **it's a cache, so the hit ratio is everything**: latency, cost, and origin protection all flow from the fraction of requests answered at the edge without touching the origin — so cache-key design, TTLs, and immutable URLs (which maximize hits) are first-order concerns, not tuning. Second, **there is a fundamental tension between freshness and offload**: caching longer offloads the origin more but risks serving stale content, so the system needs explicit control over that trade-off — TTL-bounded staleness for most content and active purge when a change must propagate fast. Third, **it must stay available and fast under failure**: a PoP going down, an origin becoming unreachable, or a traffic flood are normal operating conditions at this scale, so routing must steer around dead PoPs, the edge must be able to serve **stale content when the origin is down**, and the distributed edge must **absorb** attacks rather than pass them through. Note what's **relaxed**: content is usually not personalized (so it's shareable across users — the thing that makes caching work at all), and consistency is **eventual and bounded** (a cached copy may be seconds-to-minutes stale, which is acceptable for the overwhelming majority of web content). The system trades strict global freshness for enormous origin offload and low latency, and manages that trade-off explicitly.

The takeaways. First — the point of the entire system — **the cache-hit ratio determines origin load.** At **20 million requests/sec** hitting the edge and a **95% hit ratio**, the origin sees only the **5% that miss** — **1 million requests/sec**, a **20× reduction**. The edge serves **2000 GB/s** of egress while the origin must supply only **100 GB/s**. A CDN's value is exactly this multiplier: it lets a modest origin sit behind a firehose. Second — the crucial, counterintuitive part — **the lever is non-linear because origin traffic scales with the *miss* ratio, not the hit ratio.** Raising the hit ratio from 95% to **99%** sounds like a marginal 4-point gain, but it takes the miss ratio from 5% to 1% — a **fifth** — so origin load drops another **5×**, from 1M to **200k requests/sec**. The last few percent of hit ratio are worth far more at the origin than they look, which is why the design *obsesses* over anything that lifts the hit ratio: disciplined cache keys, long TTLs, immutable URLs, and an origin shield. Third, **the edge must be built for the full firehose** — 20M req/s and 2000 GB/s spread across hundreds of PoPs and thousands of edge servers — while the **origin is sized for the trickle** that gets through. The capacity story sets the whole agenda: maximize hits (cheap latency, cheap bytes, protected origin) and treat every miss as expensive, because in aggregate the misses are what can crush the one origin behind you.

Two ideas complete the picture. The **cache key** is the single most consequential design choice in the whole system: make it too *specific* (include a cookie, a tracking query param, a per-user header) and the same object fragments into millions of distinct entries, collapsing the hit ratio; make it too *loose* and you serve the wrong variant to someone. And the **origin config is the durable source of truth about behavior**, while the cached bytes are throwaway — the discipline throughout is that **edges cache derived copies governed by centrally-pushed config**, so any edge can be rebuilt from the origin and the control plane at any time.

<h2 id="high-level-architecture">High-Level Architecture</h2>

The architecture is a **delivery pipeline** from user to origin with caches at every hop, plus a **control plane** that pushes configuration and purges out to the whole fleet.

<CdnArchitecture />

- **User.** Sends a request to the CDN's hostname; gets steered to a nearby edge.
- **Request Routing.** Anycast (BGP) or GeoDNS directs the user to the **nearest healthy PoP**, routing around failures and load.
- **Edge PoP.** The cache close to the user. Serves hits directly (most traffic); on a miss, fetches upstream. Terminates TLS.
- **Origin Shield.** A mid-tier parent cache that all edges fetch through, so the origin sees one request per object instead of one per edge — the thundering-herd defense.
- **Origin.** The customer's source of truth, sized only for the miss trickle that gets past the caches.
- **Control Plane.** Pushes versioned config and purge commands to every edge; the durable brain behind the disposable edge caches.

The decisive shape is a **cache hierarchy that funnels traffic down**: a request is answered as high up (as close to the user) as possible, and only genuine misses fall through to the shield and, rarely, the origin. The data plane (routing + edges + shield) carries the firehose; the control plane (config + purge) is low-volume but governs how every edge behaves. Every section that follows elaborates one piece: routing to the edge, the edge cache itself, origin offload via the hierarchy and shield, cache invalidation, dynamic content, and edge security.

<h2 id="request-routing">Request Routing to the Edge</h2>

Before caching can help, the user has to *reach* the right edge — the nearest, healthy, not-overloaded one out of hundreds of PoPs. This is the first hard problem, and there are two dominant approaches, operating at different layers.

**Anycast** works at the network layer: the CDN announces the *same* IP address from *every* PoP via BGP, and the internet's routing fabric naturally delivers each user's packets to the **topologically nearest** PoP announcing that address. It's elegant — one address, no per-user decision, and **automatic failover**: if a PoP withdraws its route (goes down), BGP reconverges and traffic flows to the next-nearest PoP with no client change. The trade-offs: routing is **coarse** (BGP optimizes for network hops, not true latency or current load) and a route can **flip mid-session** if the network reconverges — harmless for stateless HTTP (the TCP connection simply re-establishes to the new PoP) but a reason CDNs keep edges stateless. **DNS-based routing (GeoDNS)** works at the naming layer: when the user's resolver looks up the CDN hostname, the authoritative DNS returns the IP of the **nearest/healthiest edge**, often refined with **EDNS Client Subnet** so the answer reflects the *user's* location rather than the resolver's. This gives **more control** — you can factor in real-time PoP health and load, do weighted or latency-based steering, and drain a PoP gracefully — but it's **bounded by DNS caching**: answers live for a TTL, and resolvers/clients cache them, so changes propagate with lag, and the resolver's location can be a poor proxy for the user's without ECS. Both approaches lean on continuous **health checking**: a PoP that fails checks is withdrawn (route pulled, or removed from DNS answers) so users are steered away. Real CDNs often **combine** them — Anycast for coarse, instant regional steering plus DNS for finer, health- and load-aware selection. The principle: route each user to the **nearest healthy PoP** using **Anycast (BGP, instant failover, coarse)** and/or **GeoDNS (fine, health/load-aware, DNS-TTL-bounded)**, with health checks steering traffic away from failed or overloaded edges.

<h2 id="edge-cache">The Edge Cache: Keys, TTLs & Hit Ratio</h2>

Inside a PoP is the heart of the system: a cache, and everything about how well the CDN works comes down to how that cache is keyed, filled, expired, and evicted. The **cache key** is the identity of a cached object — and it is *not* just the URL. It's a deliberate normalization of the request: scheme, host, and path, plus a **carefully chosen subset** of query parameters and headers (`Accept-Encoding` matters — you cache the gzip and Brotli variants separately; a session cookie or a `utm_*` tracking param must *not*). This choice is the highest-leverage decision in the whole design: include a volatile field and a single logical object **fragments** into thousands of near-identical entries that each get few hits, and the **hit ratio collapses**. Cache-key hygiene — strip tracking params, ignore irrelevant headers, normalize case and ordering — is how you protect the hit ratio.

**Cacheability and TTL** come from HTTP semantics: `Cache-Control: max-age` / `s-maxage` set how long an entry is fresh, `immutable` says "never revalidate," and `no-store`/`private` mark responses the shared cache must not keep. On a **hit**, the edge serves the stored bytes directly (from RAM for the hottest objects, from local **SSD** for the long tail — working sets vastly exceed RAM, so edge caches are **tiered**). On a **miss**, the edge fetches upstream, **stores** the object with its TTL, and serves it. Because capacity is finite, entries are **evicted** under pressure, typically by an **LRU-style policy** that keeps the popular objects hot — the same eviction and hot-key dynamics as the [Distributed Cache](/learn/distributed-cache), here per edge server. The one number that summarizes the cache's health is the **cache-hit ratio**, the headline KPI: as the capacity section showed, a few points of hit ratio translate into a multiplied change in origin load. The principle: design the **cache key** to be as *coarse* as correctness allows (so shareable objects share an entry and the hit ratio stays high), drive **cacheability/TTL** from `Cache-Control`, serve hits from **tiered RAM→SSD** storage with **LRU eviction**, and treat the **cache-hit ratio** as the metric that governs latency, cost, and origin protection.

<h2 id="origin-offload">Origin Offload: Hierarchy & Shield</h2>

The capacity section framed origin offload as the goal; this section is about the failure mode that threatens it. Consider a popular object whose TTL just expired, or a new object that's suddenly hot: across the fleet, **thousands of edges all miss at the same moment** and all reach for the origin simultaneously. That synchronized stampede — a **cache stampede** or **thundering herd** — can hit the origin with orders of magnitude more load than it's sized for, precisely when the cache was supposed to be protecting it. It's the [Distributed Cache](/learn/distributed-cache)'s stampede problem, now at global scale across independent edges. Three mechanisms defend the origin.

First, **request coalescing** (collapsed forwarding): within a single edge, many concurrent requests for the *same* missing key are collapsed into **one** upstream fetch — the first request goes to origin, the rest **wait** for its result and are all served from it. One edge, one origin fetch per object, no matter how many users asked at once. Second, an **origin shield** — a designated **mid-tier parent cache** that all edges fetch *through* instead of going to the origin directly. Now the coalescing happens again at the shield: the whole fleet's misses for an object funnel into the shield, which makes **at most one** request to the origin and fans the result back out to every edge. The hierarchy turns "thousands of edges → origin" into "thousands of edges → one shield → one origin fetch." Third, **stale-while-revalidate**: rather than blocking on a refresh when an entry expires, the edge serves the **slightly stale** copy immediately and refreshes it in the background with a single request — users never wait, and the origin sees one revalidation instead of a herd. Together these make origin load depend on the number of *distinct objects* fetched, not the number of users — which is what lets a small origin sit behind a 20M-req/s edge. The principle: protect the single origin from the thundering herd of synchronized misses with **request coalescing** (one upstream fetch per object per node), an **origin-shield hierarchy** (the fleet's misses funnel through one parent cache), and **stale-while-revalidate** (serve stale, refresh once in the background) — so the origin's load scales with distinct content, not with traffic.

<h2 id="cache-invalidation">Cache Invalidation & Freshness</h2>

"There are only two hard things in computer science: cache invalidation and naming things." A CDN lives the first one at scale: the same object is cached across **thousands of independent edges**, and when it changes at the origin, every one of those copies is now potentially wrong. There are three strategies, and mature CDNs use all three for different content.

First, **TTL-based expiry with revalidation** — the default and the workhorse. Each entry carries a TTL (`max-age`), and once it expires the edge doesn't blindly refetch; it **revalidates** with a conditional request (`If-None-Match: <etag>` or `If-Modified-Since`). If the origin's copy is unchanged it returns a tiny **`304 Not Modified`** and the edge extends the entry's life — cheap, no body transferred. This is **eventually consistent with bounded staleness**: content can be at most one TTL stale, which for the vast majority of web content is exactly the right trade-off (offload a lot, tolerate seconds-to-minutes of lag). Second, **active purge / invalidation** — when a change *must* propagate fast (a wrong price, a legal takedown, a deploy). The control plane pushes a **purge** to every edge, targeting a single URL, a **path/wildcard**, or a **surrogate/cache tag** (invalidate every object tagged `product-42` in one command — pages, images, and API responses together). Purge is fleet-wide and **eventually consistent with real propagation latency**: it takes some time to reach all hundreds of PoPs, and the system exposes that. Third — and the best answer when you can use it — **versioned immutable URLs**: put a **content hash** in the filename (`app.3f9a2c.js`), mark it `Cache-Control: immutable`, and cache it *forever*. When the content changes, the hash changes, so it's a **new URL** that was never cached — you **never invalidate at all**, you just publish a new name and update the reference. This is the same trick [Pastebin](/learn/pastebin) and [Photo Sharing](/learn/photo-sharing) use for immutable blobs, and it's why static assets get year-long TTLs safely. The principle: default to **TTL + conditional revalidation (304)** for bounded-staleness freshness, reach for **active purge (by URL/path/tag, fleet-propagated)** when a change must be immediate, and prefer **versioned immutable URLs** wherever possible so invalidation is designed away entirely.

<h2 id="dynamic-edge">Beyond Static: Dynamic & Edge Compute</h2>

So far the content has been cacheable and shared. But much of the modern web is **dynamic or personalized** — a logged-in dashboard, a search result, an API response — and looks uncacheable. A CDN still helps, in three ways, and increasingly does real computation at the edge.

First, **connection and route optimization**, which accelerates even fully uncacheable requests. The edge **terminates TLS and the user's TCP connection** right next to them (a fast local handshake instead of a slow round-trip to a distant origin), then carries the request to the origin over **warm, pre-established, optimized backbone connections** the CDN keeps hot. The user's slow last mile is short; the long haul runs on a tuned private network. Second, **micro-caching**: even "dynamic" endpoints are often identical for many users over a very short window, so caching an "uncacheable" response for even **one second** can absorb enormous load on a hot endpoint (a thousand requests/sec for the same trending page become one origin hit per second) with staleness no one notices. Third, **edge compute**: run application logic *at the edge* — lightweight functions for personalization, authentication, header rewrites, A/B routing, or request filtering — so decisions happen close to the user without a round trip to origin. A powerful pattern here is **edge-side includes (ESI)**: cache the static shell of a page aggressively and compose the small dynamic fragments at the edge, so a mostly-static page is still mostly served from cache with only its personalized bits assembled per request. The principle: a CDN accelerates dynamic content even when it can't cache the *body* — by **terminating TLS/TCP at the edge over a warm backbone**, **micro-caching** hot endpoints for seconds, and running **edge compute / ESI** to do personalization and composition close to the user — extending the CDN from a static cache into a distributed application edge.

<h2 id="edge-security">Security at the Edge</h2>

Because the CDN sits **in front of everything** and commands enormous, geographically distributed capacity, it's the natural place to be the **security perimeter** — often the main reason a company adopts one. Three capabilities matter. **TLS termination at the edge**: the CDN presents the certificate and completes the handshake close to the user, so encryption setup is fast (session resumption, modern ciphers) and the origin is relieved of per-user TLS cost; the CDN then re-encrypts to the origin over its backbone. **DDoS absorption**: a single origin can be flattened by a volumetric flood, but the CDN's distributed edge has **aggregate capacity far larger than any one attacker's target**, and **Anycast spreads a flood across every PoP** rather than concentrating it — so the network *soaks* attacks at the edge, filtering malicious packets before they ever reach the origin. This is the clearest expression of the whole design philosophy: the edge is a shield. **WAF, rate limiting, and bot management**: because every request already passes through the edge, it's the ideal choke point to inspect and filter — block SQL-injection and XSS payloads with a **web application firewall**, throttle abusive clients with **edge rate limiting** (the [Rate Limiter](/learn/rate-limiter) enforced globally at the edge, before traffic reaches origin), and challenge or block bots. The principle: make the edge the **security perimeter** — **terminate TLS** near the user, **absorb DDoS** with distributed Anycast capacity, and run **WAF / rate limiting / bot management** at the edge so malicious traffic is filtered before it ever reaches the origin, turning the CDN into a shield as much as an accelerator.

<Callout variant="info" title="The edge is a shield, not just an accelerator">
The same properties that make a CDN fast make it a security perimeter. Its distributed capacity dwarfs a single origin's, so it **absorbs** volumetric DDoS that would flatten the origin, and Anycast spreads the flood across PoPs instead of concentrating it. Because every request already transits the edge, that's the cheapest place to terminate TLS, run a WAF, and rate-limit abusive clients — filtering bad traffic before it consumes any origin resources. Many organizations put a CDN in front of even *uncacheable* apps purely for this protection.
</Callout>

<h2 id="scalability-evolution">Scalability & Evolution</h2>

Don't build the global, sharded, edge-compute platform on day one. Each stage answers a specific pressure and adds a specific cost — name the trigger before taking on the complexity.

The progression tracks the wall you're hitting. A **single reverse-proxy cache** (nginx or Varnish in front of the origin) offloads reads for one region and is the right first step, but does nothing for users on other continents. Going **multi-PoP with geo-routing** is what makes it a CDN: edge caches in many regions plus Anycast/GeoDNS so each user hits a nearby one — now latency is low globally, at the cost of running a worldwide fleet. As that fleet grows, its **aggregate misses stampede the origin**, so you add a **cache hierarchy with an origin shield** and request coalescing, making origin load a function of distinct content rather than edge count — this is where most of the hard design lives and where the recommended stage sits. Finally, as content gets dynamic and attacks arrive, the edge becomes a **programmable, defended platform**: edge compute and ESI for dynamic acceleration, micro-caching for hot endpoints, and TLS/DDoS/WAF for security. Most of a CDN's value and difficulty is captured by "multi-PoP edge caches with geo-routing and an origin-shield hierarchy."

<h2 id="resiliency-failure-modes">Resiliency & Failure Modes</h2>

Reason about every dependency in the same matrix — blast radius, detection, mitigation, recovery. The CDN's guiding rule: **the edge is disposable and self-healing, the origin must be shielded, and serving slightly stale beats serving an error.**

<FailureMatrix
  rows={[
    { failure: "Edge PoP failure", impact: "Users on that PoP lose their nearby edge", detection: "Health checks fail", mitigation: "Anycast/DNS route around it to the next-nearest PoP", recovery: "PoP returns; routing reconverges; cache refills lazily" },
    { failure: "Origin unreachable", impact: "Misses can't be filled", detection: "Origin fetch errors/timeouts", mitigation: "Serve stale-if-error (keep serving expired copies); shield absorbs", recovery: "Origin returns; entries revalidate on next expiry" },

    { failure: "DDoS flood", impact: "Attack traffic aimed at origin", detection: "Traffic anomaly detection", mitigation: "Absorb at the edge; Anycast spreads it; WAF/rate-limit filters", recovery: "Flood filtered before origin; origin unaffected" },

  ]}
/>

The theme: **let the edge fail freely, protect the origin absolutely, and prefer stale over error.** A PoP failure is nearly a non-event — routing steers around it and its cache (disposable, derived) refills lazily on the next PoP. When the **origin is unreachable**, the edge keeps serving **stale-if-error** copies so users see slightly old content instead of an outage, while the shield absorbs the misses. The **stampede** — the scariest origin threat — is contained by coalescing, the shield, and stale-while-revalidate, so a synchronized expiry becomes a single refill. A **wrong cached entry** is fixed by fleet-wide **purge** (and a cache-key fix so it doesn't recur). **Config and purge propagation** is made reliable with versioned, gradually-rolled-out config and per-edge acknowledgment, so a lagging edge is detected and re-pushed. A **DDoS** is exactly what the distributed edge is built to **absorb**. And a **cold cache** fills through the shield rather than hammering the origin. Because the edge is throwaway and the origin is precious, recovery is "reroute and let the cache refill" on the volatile side and "shield, serve stale, and never pass the flood through" on the protected side.

<h2 id="tradeoffs-alternatives">Trade-offs & Alternatives</h2>

Every recommendation here bought something and gave up something. Stating both is the difference between an answer and a strong answer.

<DecisionRecord
  title="The one-sentence design"
  choice="Route each user to the nearest healthy PoP with Anycast and/or GeoDNS (health-checked); serve from a tiered RAM→SSD edge cache keyed by a deliberately coarse cache key (URL plus only the query/headers that change the response) to maximize the hit ratio; protect a single origin from the thundering herd with request coalescing, an origin-shield mid-tier hierarchy, and stale-while-revalidate; keep content fresh with TTL + conditional revalidation (304) by default, active purge (by URL/path/surrogate-tag, fleet-propagated) when a change must be immediate, and versioned immutable URLs wherever possible so invalidation is designed away; accelerate dynamic content with edge TLS/TCP termination over a warm backbone, micro-caching, and edge compute/ESI; and make the edge the security perimeter with TLS termination, DDoS absorption, and edge WAF/rate limiting."
  rationale="A CDN is a globally distributed cache whose entire value is origin offload, and origin load scales with the miss ratio — so the design maximizes the hit ratio (coarse keys, long TTLs, immutable URLs) and funnels the misses that remain through a hierarchy that dedupes them (coalescing + shield), letting a small origin sit behind a firehose. Routing gets users to a nearby healthy edge; the edge is stateless and disposable so PoP loss and route flips are non-events.   alternatives="No CDN (serve everything from origin) — simple but slow globally and fragile under load.   revisitWhen="Content becomes highly personalized (caching helps less; lean on edge compute/ESI and connection acceleration). Freshness requirements tighten toward real-time (invest in faster purge propagation or push invalidation). You need programmable logic at the edge (a full edge-compute runtime). />

Four axes capture the real tension:

- **Anycast vs. DNS routing.** Anycast gives instant, automatic failover but coarse, load-blind steering; GeoDNS gives fine, health/load-aware control but is bounded by DNS caching. Combine them — Anycast for failover, DNS for finesse.
- **TTL vs. active purge vs. immutable URLs.** TTL is cheap and eventual (bounded staleness); purge is immediate but propagates fleet-wide with latency; immutable URLs avoid invalidation entirely. Default to TTL, purge when urgent, prefer immutable URLs.
- **Pull/lazy-fill vs. push/pre-warm.** Pull fills the cache on demand (simple, storage-efficient, cold-start misses); push pre-warms edges (great hit ratio, huge storage, mostly wasted). Pull by default, pre-warm known hot objects.
- **Origin shield vs. direct-to-origin.** A shield adds a hop and a component but collapses fleet-wide misses to one origin fetch; direct is simpler but stampedes the origin. Use a shield at scale.
