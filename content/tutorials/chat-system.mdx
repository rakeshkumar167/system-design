<h2 id="interview-framing">Interview Framing</h2>

A chat system delivers messages between people in real time: you type a message, your friend sees it a moment later, you see "delivered" and then "read", and if they were offline they get it the next time they open the app. WhatsApp, Messenger, Slack DMs, iMessage, Telegram — all the same core system, scaled to a billion users and group chats. From the outside it looks like a CRUD app over a `messages` table: POST a message, store it, the recipient GETs their messages. That framing produces something that works in a demo and is fundamentally wrong at scale, because the recipient can't keep asking "anything for me?" — the message has to **arrive at their device the instant it's sent**, which a request/response model can't do. The server has to **push**, and pushing to a billion devices is a different kind of system entirely.

The hard part is **real-time delivery to recipients who may be online or offline, in order, exactly once as they see it, over millions of persistent connections**. Three problems dominate, and storing the message is the easy one. First, the **connection layer**: real-time push requires a **persistent, bidirectional connection** — a **WebSocket** — held open for every online user, on a fleet of **stateful gateway servers**. And because a user is connected to exactly *one* gateway, delivering a message means finding *which* gateway holds the recipient's connection (a user→gateway **session registry**) and routing to it — you can't just put stateless boxes behind a load balancer. Second, **delivery and guarantees**: an **online** recipient gets a real-time push; an **offline** recipient has the message saved in a per-user **mailbox** and delivered when they reconnect; and because networks drop packets, delivery is **at-least-once** with client **ACKs** and **dedup by a client-generated message id**, which drives the **sent → delivered → read** receipts. Third, **ordering**: everyone in a conversation must see messages in the *same* order, so each message gets a **monotonic per-conversation sequence number** (you can't trust wall-clocks across devices), and a **group** message fans out to every member. Plus **presence** (online / last-seen / typing), which is cheap per event but brutal in fan-out. The reframe to lead with: *it looks like storing and fetching messages, but the server can't wait to be asked — it must push over a persistent connection, so the design is a stateful fleet of WebSocket gateways holding millions of connections, a session registry that routes each message to the recipient's gateway, an online-push / offline-mailbox delivery model with ACK-based at-least-once guarantees, per-conversation ordering, and presence. Persisting a message is trivial; delivering it in real time to whichever of a billion users is connected where, in order, exactly once as they see it, is the design.*

Spend the opening minutes scoping. Good questions to ask:

- 1:1 only, or group chats too? Groups add fan-out and per-member delivery state.
- How real-time — sub-second push, or is a short delay fine? This decides WebSockets vs polling.
- Do we need delivery and read receipts, and presence/typing? They add ACK propagation and ephemeral state.
- Offline support and history — must messages survive when the recipient is offline for days? Yes → durable mailbox + history.
- How big — DAU and peak concurrency? The concurrent-connection count is the real sizing driver.

Three targets shape every later decision. First, **real-time delivery requires server push**, which means a **persistent connection** per online user — the system's defining feature and the reason it's built around a stateful gateway fleet rather than stateless request/response. Second, **reliability is non-negotiable and networks are unreliable**, so delivery is **at-least-once** (retry until the client acknowledges) combined with **idempotent dedup** by a client-generated message id, giving the user **exactly-once display** even though the wire protocol may redeliver. The system trades global ordering and perfect presence for reliable, ordered, real-time delivery within each conversation, at connection scale.

Three supporting entities complete the picture. <h2 id="high-level-architecture">High-Level Architecture</h2>

The architecture is built around the **persistent connection**: clients hold a socket to a **stateful gateway**, and a **message service** persists each message and **routes** it — through a **session registry** — to the recipient's gateway for a real-time push, or to their **mailbox** if they're offline.

<ChatArchitecture />

- **Sender / Recipient (clients).** Hold a persistent WebSocket to a gateway; send and receive framed events; reconnect with backoff and sync by seq.
- **WebSocket Gateway.** A stateful server that holds millions of open connections, authenticates them, runs heartbeats, and pushes messages to the clients connected to it. Sized by connections-per-server.
- **Message Service.** Assigns the per-conversation seq, persists the message, looks up the recipient's gateway, and routes the message (or writes it to the mailbox). The brain of delivery.
- **Message Store.** Durable home of all messages and history (persist-before-deliver), partitioned by conversation.
- **Session Registry.** The fast, ephemeral user→gateway mapping used to route a message to the one gateway holding the recipient's connection.
- **Mailbox.** A per-user durable queue of messages for an **offline** recipient, drained on reconnect.
- **Presence.** Tracks online / last-seen via heartbeats and fans presence changes out to subscribers.

The decisive boundary is **the gateways hold the connections, and the message service routes between them via the session registry** — everything else is persistence and offline handling around that core. The gateway fleet absorbs the millions of stateful sockets; the session registry turns "deliver to user X" into "push to gateway gw-734"; the message service persists first (durability) then delivers (push or mailbox); and presence rides alongside as ephemeral state. When the recipient is offline, the mailbox makes delivery reliable; when they're online, the registry makes it instant. Every section that follows elaborates one piece: the connection layer and routing, the delivery model and mailbox, the guarantees and receipts, ordering, group fan-out, and presence.

<h2 id="detailed-flows">Detailed Flows</h2>

Four flows cover the system: sending to an online recipient, delivering to an offline one, propagating receipts, and updating presence. The first two are the core delivery paths; the others are what make delivery feel complete and alive.

**Send a message to an online recipient.** The sender emits the message over its socket; the gateway forwards it to the message service, which assigns a seq, persists it, looks up the recipient's gateway in the session registry, and routes it there for an immediate push. This is the real-time happy path — persist, locate, push.

**Deliver to an offline recipient.** If the recipient has no live connection, the message service writes the message into their per-user mailbox; when they later reconnect, the client syncs (by last-seen seq) and the mailbox is drained to their device. This is how "deliver later" stays reliable.

**Propagate a delivery receipt.** When the recipient's device receives a message it ACKs **delivered** (and **read** when the user opens it); the ACK flows back through the gateway and message service, which updates the message status and notifies the sender — the "✓✓" you see. This is the receipt state machine in motion.

<h2 id="connection-layer">WebSockets & Connection Management</h2>

The connection layer is the centerpiece, and it starts with *why* you can't use plain request/response. To get a message to a recipient the instant it's sent, the **server must initiate** the transfer — but HTTP is client-initiated, so the recipient would have to **poll** ("anything new?") constantly, which is wasteful and laggy, or hold a **long-poll** open, which is a hack. The right tool is a **WebSocket**: a single TCP connection, upgraded once, that stays **open and bidirectional**, so the server can push a frame to the client at any moment with no request. Every online user holds one such persistent connection to a **gateway server**, and because connections are long-lived and stateful, the gateways are sized by **how many connections each can hold** (tens to hundreds of thousands), giving the ~1,000-server fleet from the capacity model.

The subtle, load-bearing piece is **routing**. A user is connected to **exactly one** gateway, and the message service has no way to know which one without help — so a **session registry** records `user → gateway` on connect and is consulted on every delivery: "user X is on gw-734, push there." This is why chat can't be stateless: the connection *is* state, pinned to a server. Around this sit the **lifecycle** concerns: **authenticate** on connect (then trust the socket), exchange **heartbeats** (ping/pong) to detect dead connections and keep NATs open, **reconnect with backoff** when a socket drops (re-registering in the session registry, possibly on a *different* gateway, and syncing missed messages by seq), and **drain gracefully** when a gateway is deployed or dies (clients reconnect elsewhere). The principle: use **WebSockets** for server push, hold the connections on a **stateful gateway fleet** sized by connections-per-server, and make delivery possible by maintaining a **session registry** that maps each user to their current gateway — with a lifecycle of auth, heartbeats, and reconnect-and-resync that treats dropped connections as normal.

<h2 id="message-delivery">Message Delivery & the Mailbox</h2>

With connections and routing in place, delivery has two paths depending on whether the recipient is **online**. The **online path**: the message service, after persisting the message, looks up the recipient in the **session registry**, finds their gateway, and **routes** the message there for an immediate **push** down the open socket — sub-second delivery. The **offline path**: there's no live connection to push to, so the message is written to the recipient's **mailbox** — a per-user **durable queue** of undelivered messages — and simply waits. When the recipient next connects, their client **syncs** (asks for everything after its last-seen seq), the mailbox is **drained** to the device, and they catch up. Critically, the message is **persisted to the message store first**, *before* either delivery attempt, so a crash mid-delivery never loses it — delivery is a best-effort push over durable state, not the system of record.

This split is what makes chat reliable across the messy reality of mobile: people are constantly going offline (subway, airplane, dead battery) and back, and the mailbox absorbs all of it. For **group** messages, the same logic runs per member — push to those online, mailbox for those offline. And when a recipient is offline, the system can hand off to the **Notification Service** to send an out-of-app **push notification** ("You have a new message"), nudging them to reopen the app — a clean separation where chat owns in-app delivery and the notification system owns the out-of-app nudge. The principle: **persist first, then deliver** by **pushing to online recipients** (via the session registry) and **queuing for offline ones** in a durable **mailbox** drained on reconnect — with the **Notification Service** handling the out-of-app push, so a message is never lost regardless of whether anyone is connected.

<h2 id="delivery-guarantees">Delivery Guarantees & Receipts</h2>

Networks are unreliable — packets drop, sockets die mid-send, a phone loses signal right as a message arrives — so the delivery guarantee is **at-least-once**: the server (or sender) keeps the message pending and **retries** until it receives an **ACK** from the recipient's device confirming receipt. At-least-once means a message can be **delivered twice** (a retry races an ACK that was already in flight), which would show duplicates — so the client **dedups** by the **client-generated `message_id`**: if it's already seen that id, it drops the redelivery. The combination — **at-least-once on the wire + idempotent dedup by message_id** — gives the user **exactly-once display**, which is what they actually care about. (True end-to-end exactly-once delivery is impossible over an unreliable network; at-least-once-plus-dedup is how every real system fakes it.)

Riding on the same ACKs is the **receipt state machine**: a message moves **sent** (accepted and persisted by the server) → **delivered** (the recipient's device ACKed receipt) → **read** (the recipient opened the conversation and the client sent a read-ACK). Each transition is an event propagated **back to the sender**, which is how you see one check, two checks, two blue checks. The receipts are themselves small messages, deduped and ordered like any other, and `read` is often privacy-gated (users can disable read receipts). The principle: guarantee delivery with **at-least-once + retries until ACK**, make it **exactly-once as the user sees it** via **idempotent dedup on the client-generated message_id**, and drive the **sent → delivered → read** receipts off the ACKs, propagating each back to the sender.

<h2 id="ordering-consistency">Ordering & Consistency</h2>

Within a conversation, everyone must see messages in the **same order** — a reply must come after the message it answers, for both people — and you cannot get this from **timestamps**, because device and server clocks **skew** and two messages can carry misleading times. The fix is a **monotonic per-conversation sequence number**: when the message service accepts a message, it assigns the next `seq` for that conversation, and **all clients order by `seq`**, never by wall-clock. The **conversation is the ordering unit** (and a natural **partition key**): a single sequencer per conversation serializes its messages, so concurrent sends from two people are simply assigned consecutive seq values in whatever order the sequencer accepts them — a total order everyone agrees on. Cross-conversation order doesn't matter, so different conversations are independent and scale horizontally by partition.

This makes **gaps detectable and recoverable**. Because seq is contiguous per conversation, a client that has up to seq 4096 and suddenly receives 4099 knows it **missed 4097–4098** and re-syncs (`GET …?after_seq=4096`) to fill the hole — the same mechanism that drains the mailbox on reconnect. Ordering and catch-up are thus the same idea: trust the seq, detect gaps, pull what's missing. (Group chats keep a single per-conversation seq so all members share one order; the sequencer for a hot group is a point to scale carefully.) The principle: order by a **monotonic per-conversation sequence number** assigned by the server — not by clocks — with the **conversation as the ordering/partition unit**, so all participants agree on one order, concurrent sends are serialized, and **missing seqs are detected and re-synced**.

<h2 id="group-chat-fanout">Group Chat & Fan-out</h2>

A group message must reach **every member**, which is **fan-out on write** — the same idea as the **News Feed**, applied to messaging: the message service takes one accepted message and delivers a copy to each member, **pushing** to those currently online (via their gateways) and writing to the **mailbox** of those offline. The message is stored **once** (keyed by the group conversation) and fanned out as **per-member delivery**, with **per-member delivery state** (each member has their own delivered/read status) — which is why a group's read receipts show "read by 4 of 7." For **small groups** (a few to a few dozen) this is cheap and indistinguishable from 1:1 delivery repeated.

The interesting case is **large groups** — thousands of members, or broadcast-style channels — which are the chat analog of the News Feed's **celebrity** problem: one message becomes thousands of pushes and mailbox writes, and a chatty large group multiplies that per message. The mitigations rhyme with fan-out everywhere: **bound** the fan-out work, do it **asynchronously** through a queue so a burst doesn't stall the sender, and for very large broadcast groups consider **pull** (members fetch the group's recent messages on open) instead of pushing to everyone, or tiered delivery (push to active members, mailbox/pull for the rest). All of it preserves the single per-conversation **seq** so every member sees one order. The principle: deliver group messages by **fan-out on write** — one stored message, per-member push-or-mailbox with per-member delivery state — keeping small groups trivial and taming **large groups** with bounded, async fan-out (and pull for broadcast-scale), exactly the hot-user trade-off the News Feed makes.

<h2 id="presence-typing">Presence, Typing & Last-Seen</h2>

**Presence** (online / offline / last-seen) and **typing** indicators feel minor but have a distinctive cost profile: they are **ephemeral** and **high-frequency**, and their expense is **fan-out**, not storage. Presence is driven by **heartbeats** — the same keepalives that detect dead connections double as "I'm still here" signals — so a user's online state is just "have we heard a heartbeat recently," held in a **fast, ephemeral store** (not the durable message path), and **last-seen** is a timestamp updated on heartbeat or disconnect. Typing is even more transient: a short-lived "user is typing" event that auto-expires after a couple of seconds. None of it is persisted as history.

The challenge is that **every presence change must notify everyone watching that user**, and that fan-out **amplifies**: a user with many contacts going online can trigger a notification to each, and typing indicators fire repeatedly as someone types. Naively this can generate more traffic than the actual messages. So presence is aggressively **optimized**: **throttle and batch** updates (don't emit every keystroke; coalesce), **scope** delivery to where it matters (only push presence/typing for conversations a user currently has **open**, not their entire contact list), and treat it as **best-effort and eventually consistent** — a last-seen that's a few seconds stale is fine, and presence is the **first thing to shed** when the system is under load. The principle: model presence and typing as **ephemeral, heartbeat-driven** state kept out of the durable path, and control their **fan-out cost** by throttling, batching, and scoping to active conversations — accepting eventual consistency and shedding presence before ever degrading message delivery.

<h2 id="scalability-evolution">Scalability & Evolution</h2>

Don't build the globally-distributed, sharded-mailbox system on day one. Each stage answers a specific pressure and adds a specific cost — name the trigger before taking on the complexity.

<TradeoffTable
  columns={["Stage", "Trigger", "Cost"]}
  rows={[
    ["HTTP polling", "A toy app, few users", "Laggy and wasteful — clients poll constantly; no real-time push"],
    ["Long-polling", "Want push-ish without WebSockets", "A hack — held requests, awkward server resources, still not full-duplex"],
    ["WebSocket gateways + session registry + mailbox", "Real-time at scale; offline users", "Stateful gateway fleet; route via session registry; durable mailboxes"],
    ["Globally distributed gateways, sharded mailboxes, regional presence", "A billion users worldwide", "Cross-region routing; sharded mailbox/seq; regional presence; Notification Service for offline push"],
  ]}
  recommendedRow={2}
/>

The progression tracks the wall you're hitting. **HTTP polling** is the naive start — clients ask "anything new?" on a timer — and it's both laggy and wasteful, useless for real-time. **Long-polling** holds a request open to fake push, but it's a hack that ties up server resources and still isn't truly bidirectional. The center of gravity for a real chat system is **WebSocket gateways with a session registry and durable mailboxes** — this is where push is real, routing works across a stateful fleet, and offline users are handled reliably. Only true global scale needs **geo-distributed gateways with sharded mailboxes and regional presence**, paying for cross-region routing, partitioned per-conversation sequencing, and regional presence — leaning on the **Notification Service** for the offline out-of-app push. Most chat systems are well-served by "WebSocket gateways, session-registry routing, durable mailbox, at-least-once + dedup."

<h2 id="resiliency-failure-modes">Resiliency & Failure Modes</h2>

Reason about every dependency in the same matrix — blast radius, detection, mitigation, recovery. The chat system's guiding rules: **never lose or duplicate a message as the user sees it**, and **shed presence before ever degrading message delivery**.

The theme: **persist first, reconnect-and-resync, dedup, and protect messaging over presence.** A dead gateway is survivable because connections are *expected* to drop — clients reconnect to another gateway, re-register, and **sync by seq**, and any in-flight messages are **redelivered** (at-least-once) with the client **deduping** repeats. The session registry is **ephemeral and rebuildable** from reconnections, so losing it degrades but doesn't lose data. The cardinal **persist-before-deliver** rule means a store outage causes a *rejected send* (the user sees it didn't go through and retries idempotently) rather than a silent loss. Mailbox backlogs just delay offline delivery, which the reconnect-sync drains. Duplicates and gaps are handled by the two invariants that run through the whole design — **dedup by message_id** and **order/resync by seq**. And presence, being ephemeral and fan-out-heavy, is the **first thing shed** under load, never taking message delivery down with it. Recovery is almost always "reconnect, resync by seq, retry idempotently."

<h2 id="tradeoffs-alternatives">Trade-offs & Alternatives</h2>

Every recommendation here bought something and gave up something. Stating both is the difference between an answer and a strong answer.

<DecisionRecord
  title="The one-sentence design"
  choice="Hold a persistent WebSocket per online user on a stateful gateway fleet, route each message to the recipient's gateway via a user→gateway session registry, persist before delivering, push to online recipients and queue for offline ones in a durable per-user mailbox drained on reconnect, guarantee delivery at-least-once with retries-until-ACK and idempotent client dedup by a client-generated message_id (exactly-once display), order by a monotonic per-conversation sequence number, fan group messages out on write with per-member delivery state, and treat presence/typing as ephemeral heartbeat-driven state controlled by throttling and scoping — handing offline out-of-app pushes to the Notification Service."
  rationale="Real-time push mandates persistent connections, which makes the system stateful and forces the session registry for routing — the core departure from request/response. Persist-before-deliver plus an offline mailbox makes delivery reliable across mobile's constant disconnects. At-least-once + dedup is the only honest way to get exactly-once display over an unreliable network. A per-conversation seq gives a consistent order without trusting clocks and doubles as gap-detection for resync. Group fan-out reuses the News Feed pattern; presence is cheap per event but fan-out-heavy, so it's throttled and shed first. Each choice protects message integrity while scaling the connection layer."
  alternatives="HTTP polling / long-polling — simple but laggy, wasteful, and not truly push; stateless servers — impossible for held connections, since a connection is state pinned to a gateway; deliver-without-persisting — faster but loses messages on crash; exactly-once wire delivery — unachievable over an unreliable network, so at-least-once + dedup instead; timestamp ordering — inconsistent due to clock skew, so per-conversation seq; pushing every presence/typing event everywhere — floods the system, so throttle, batch, and scope; pushing to every member of a huge group — the celebrity problem, so bounded async fan-out or pull for broadcast scale."
  revisitWhen="Connection counts or per-conversation throughput outgrow a single sequencer/region and need sharding or geo-distribution; end-to-end encryption is required (changes where messages can be processed and how fan-out works); very large broadcast groups dominate (shift from push to pull); or media-heavy messaging makes the blob pipeline, not the connection layer, the bottleneck."
/>

Four axes capture the real tension:

- **WebSocket vs polling/long-poll.** WebSockets give true bidirectional push at the cost of stateful gateways and connection management; polling is stateless and simple but laggy and wasteful. At scale, WebSockets win decisively.
- **At-least-once + dedup vs exactly-once.** Exactly-once delivery is impossible over unreliable networks; at-least-once with idempotent dedup by message_id achieves exactly-once *display*, which is what matters. This is the standard, honest trade.
- **Per-conversation seq vs global ordering.** A per-conversation sequence gives consistent order where it matters and partitions cleanly; global ordering across all conversations is unnecessary and wouldn't scale. Scope ordering to the conversation.
- **Presence fan-out vs cost.** Rich, instant presence/typing is delightful but its fan-out can exceed message traffic, so it's throttled, batched, scoped to open conversations, and shed first under load — trading some immediacy for protecting message delivery.
