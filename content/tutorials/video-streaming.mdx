<h2 id="interview-framing">Interview Framing</h2>

A video streaming platform takes a file a creator uploads, turns it into something that plays smoothly on any screen and any network on Earth, and serves it to millions of people at once. A user picks a video and it starts in a second or two and never stutters; behind that simple experience are two systems with opposite personalities bolted together. The trap is to treat this as upload-and-playback CRUD — store the file, hand it back on request. The real problem is that **the ingest side and the delivery side could not be more different**, and the whole design is about building each correctly and connecting them through a seam.

On ingest, a single uploaded file is useless as-is: a phone can't smoothly play a 4K source over a weak connection, and a TV shouldn't be served a tiny low-res copy. So the platform **transcodes** every upload into a ladder of bitrates and resolutions, chops each into small segments, and writes a manifest — a slow, compute-heavy, *asynchronous* batch job that can take minutes per video. On delivery, those segments are read back at enormous scale and tight latency: peak egress is tens of terabits per second, which no origin can serve, so a **CDN** carries essentially all of it and the game becomes cache hit ratio. The reframe to lead with: *it looks like upload and playback, but the hard part is an asymmetric pipeline — an expensive async transcode fan-out feeding a CDN-dominated read path — plus adaptive bitrate streaming as the protocol that lets a client glide between qualities without rebuffering.*

Spend the opening minutes scoping. Good questions to ask:

- Video-on-demand (uploaded, then watched later) or live streaming? They share the delivery half but ingest is completely different — pin this first.
- How fast must playback start, and how much rebuffering is acceptable? (Startup latency and rebuffer ratio are the playback SLOs.)
- What's the catalogue and audience scale — thousands of creators, or a billion-hour-a-day platform?
- How skewed is popularity? A few viral titles plus a long cold tail changes the caching story.
- What devices and networks must we serve? (That decides how many rungs the bitrate ladder needs.)

This tutorial commits to concrete assumptions so the design stays consistent end to end. **In scope:** resumable upload and ingestion, the asynchronous transcoding pipeline (the rendition ladder, segmentation, parallelism), adaptive bitrate streaming (HLS/DASH manifests and segments), CDN delivery and caching, storage growth and cost, scaling, failure modes, and security/DRM. **Out of scope:** live and low-latency streaming (we design VOD and mention live as an evolution), recommendation and ranking, video codec internals, the player UI, social features, and ads — name them so the interviewer sees the boundary, then set them aside.

Three targets shape every later decision. First, **smooth playback** — fast startup and a near-zero rebuffer ratio — is the product; it's why playback is adaptive (the client drops to a lower rendition rather than freeze) and why segments are small (so quality can change every few seconds). Second, **delivery is global and CDN-served**: at tens of Tbps of peak egress, you physically cannot serve from an origin, so the CDN and its cache hit ratio are first-class requirements, not an afterthought. Third, **ingest must keep pace**: transcoding is expensive and asynchronous, and if the fleet can't drain the upload queue, a backlog grows without bound and videos take hours to go live — so throughput, autoscaling, and graceful shedding matter on the ingest side the way latency matters on the delivery side.

The takeaways. First, **ingest is steady but real**: ~83,000 hours of source video arrive per day, and the transcode fleet has to chew through every hour of it (several times over, once per rung of the ladder) or a backlog forms — ingest capacity is sized against this, not against a spike. Second, **storage multiplies**: one source becomes a *ladder* of renditions, so a 10-minute upload lands as ~0.6 GB across five renditions, and the platform adds ~**300 TB every day** — storage grows relentlessly and forever, which is why tiering and garbage-collecting failed transcodes matter. Third, and most important, **delivery is a CDN problem**: peak egress is **25 Tbps**, a number no origin can serve; with a 95% CDN cache hit ratio the origin sees only ~**1.25 Tbps**, the missed 5%. That last ratio is the whole delivery game — push the hit ratio from 95% to 98% and you've nearly halved origin load. Cheap, replicable renditions and a cache-dominated read path are exactly the inverse of Ticket Booking's precious, strongly-consistent write.

<h2 id="entity-model">Entity Model</h2>

The model centers on the **Video** as the unit a creator uploads and a viewer plays, with its `status` tracking the upload's journey through the async pipeline. The crucial idea is that the uploaded source is the durable record, and everything the player actually streams — renditions, segments, manifests — is *derived* from it and can be regenerated.

Three derived entities complete the picture. A **Rendition** is one rung of the ladder for a video (video_id, resolution, bitrate, codec, and the key of its media playlist) — a 240p, 480p, 720p, 1080p, and 4K copy might all exist. A **Segment** is a few-second chunk of one rendition's media, stored as an immutable object — these are the files the player actually downloads, and their immutability is what makes them cache forever. The **manifest** ties it together: a *master* manifest lists the renditions and their bandwidths, and each rendition has a *media* manifest (an ordered list of its segment URLs). Keeping the source separate from these derived artifacts is what lets you re-transcode (a new codec, an extra rung) without ever asking the creator to re-upload.

The **playback path is not an API call at all** in the hot sense — it's a sequence of plain HTTP GETs for the manifest and then the segments, served by the **CDN**, not your application servers. The player fetches `master.m3u8`, picks a rendition, and streams its segments (`seg_001.ts`, `seg_002.ts`, …) straight from the nearest edge. Keeping playback as cacheable static-file reads is exactly what lets a CDN absorb 25 Tbps; the moment playback required dynamic origin calls, the design would collapse under its own egress.

<h2 id="high-level-architecture">High-Level Architecture</h2>

The architecture is two pipelines that meet at object storage: an **ingest** side that turns a source into cacheable artifacts, and a **delivery** side that serves those artifacts at scale through a CDN.

<VideoStreamingArchitecture />

- **Upload service.** Stateless front door for ingest. It creates the video record, issues a presigned URL so the client uploads bytes **directly to object storage**, and — when the upload finalizes — enqueues a transcode job. It never carries the video bytes itself.
- **Raw storage (object store).** The durable home of original uploads — the system of record, at eleven-nines durability. Every rendition is derived from here, so a source can be re-transcoded any time.
- **Transcode queue + workers.** The asynchronous fan-out. A job per video is queued; a fleet of workers pulls jobs, transcodes the source into the rendition ladder, segments each rendition, writes the artifacts, and marks the video ready. The queue decouples a spiky, unbounded upload rate from a fixed, autoscaled compute fleet.
- **Segment storage.** Where the derived artifacts live — the segments and manifests the player streams. This is the **origin** the CDN pulls from on a cache miss.
- **CDN.** The delivery workhorse. Edge caches serve segments to viewers from the nearest point of presence; the origin (segment storage) is touched only on a miss. This is where the 25 Tbps actually lives.
- **Metadata DB.** Holds the video records, status, and manifest keys — small, queryable state separate from the large media blobs.

The decisive boundary is **the async seam at object storage**. The upload side writes a source and walks away; the transcode side picks it up minutes later, on its own schedule, and produces immutable artifacts; the delivery side reads those artifacts through a cache without ever knowing how they were made. Each half scales on its own axis — ingest on compute, delivery on cache/bandwidth — and neither can stall the other. Every section that follows elaborates one half of that seam.

<h2 id="detailed-flows">Detailed Flows</h2>

Four flows cover the system: uploading and ingesting a video, transcoding it, playing it back adaptively, and serving a segment through the CDN. The first two are the ingest half; the last two are the delivery half.

**Upload & ingest.** The client registers the video, then uploads bytes directly to object storage in resumable chunks. When the upload finalizes, a transcode job is enqueued and the API has already returned — the user sees "processing."

<h2 id="upload-ingestion">Upload & Ingestion</h2>

Ingestion starts with a deceptively hard problem: getting a large file off a creator's flaky connection and into durable storage. Source videos are big and uploads are long, so a single dropped connection must not mean starting over. The answer is **resumable, chunked upload** — the file is split into parts, each uploaded independently, and the client can retry just the failed part and resume where it left off. The platform issues a **presigned URL** so the bytes flow **directly from client to object storage**, never through the application servers; the API's job is to mint that URL and, later, to react to the completed upload.

This direct-to-storage path matters for the same reason the playback path bypasses the API: video bytes are enormous, and routing them through your servers would turn every upload into a bandwidth and memory problem. By handing the client a storage URL, the upload service stays small and stateless while object storage — built exactly for durable, resumable, large-object writes — does the heavy lifting.

<Callout variant="info" title="The source is the system of record">
Once the upload finalizes, that original file is the one piece of data you cannot regenerate — everything else (renditions, segments, manifests) is *derived* from it by transcoding. So it's stored durably (eleven nines) and treated as immutable. This is what makes the rest of the pipeline safe to be lossy and retryable: if a transcode job fails, corrupts an artifact, or you want to add a new rendition or adopt a better codec next year, you just re-run transcode against the untouched source. Keep the precious thing in one durable place and derive everything else.
</Callout>

When the upload completes, the service transitions the video `uploaded → transcoding` and enqueues a transcode job. That hand-off — a durable source plus a queued job — *is* the seam between the two halves of the system. The upload side's work is done; the transcode side will pick it up asynchronously. Nothing about ingest blocks on transcoding, and nothing about transcoding blocks the user's upload from completing.

<h2 id="transcoding-pipeline">Transcoding Pipeline</h2>

This is the centerpiece of the ingest half: how one uploaded file becomes something streamable everywhere. Transcoding is **compute-heavy and slow** — minutes of CPU (or GPU) per video, sometimes longer than the video's own runtime — so it absolutely cannot happen inline with the upload request. It is an **asynchronous job**: the upload enqueues work, a fleet of workers drains the queue, and the queue is what decouples a spiky, unbounded arrival rate from a fixed, autoscaled compute fleet. If uploads surge, the queue grows and workers scale out; the user never waits on a worker being free.

Each job is a **fan-out**. From one source, the worker produces a **rendition ladder** — the same content at several resolutions and bitrates (say 240p, 480p, 720p, 1080p, 4K) — so a phone on cellular and a TV on fiber can each be served an appropriate copy. Then it **segments** every rendition into small, independent chunks of a few seconds each and writes a manifest listing them. That fan-out is exactly why storage multiplied in the capacity section: one source in, a ladder of segmented renditions out.

Segmentation is what makes the chunks both **parallelizable** and **streamable**. Because segments are independent, the work can be split across many workers — different renditions, even different time ranges, transcoded concurrently — so a long video doesn't mean a long wall-clock wait. And because the player will later download those same small segments one at a time, the same chunking that speeds up transcode is what enables adaptive playback downstream.

<Callout variant="warning" title="A video is 'ready' only when its manifest is complete">
Transcode jobs fail and retry — a worker crashes mid-segment, a node is preempted, a transient error hits. The rule that keeps this safe is **atomic publish**: a video flips to `ready` only when its *entire* manifest — every rendition, every segment — exists in storage. Until then it stays `transcoding` and is unplayable. This prevents the nightmare of a half-transcoded video that plays for thirty seconds and then dead-ends at a missing segment. Make the work **idempotent** — writing segment N is keyed by (video, rendition, N), so re-running a failed job overwrites the same objects and converges rather than duplicating — and a crashed job is simply retried from the queue with no cleanup.
</Callout>

<h2 id="adaptive-bitrate-streaming">Adaptive Bitrate Streaming</h2>

Adaptive bitrate streaming (ABR) is the protocol that makes playback smooth over an unreliable, ever-changing network. The core idea is that **the client, not the server, chooses quality** — and it re-chooses constantly. Because every rendition was pre-segmented into small chunks, the player can download segment 1 at 1080p, notice the network got worse, and fetch segment 2 at 480p, then climb back up — switching rung by rung without the stream ever stopping. Standards like **HLS** and **DASH** encode this as a **master manifest** that advertises each rendition and its bandwidth, plus a **media manifest** per rendition listing its segment URLs.

Client-driven adaptation wins because only the client can see what's actually happening on the last mile. It measures the throughput of each segment download and watches its own buffer; when throughput drops or the buffer runs low, it steps down to a lower bitrate to keep playing, and when there's headroom it steps up for better quality. A server pushing a fixed bitrate is blind to the phone that just walked into an elevator — it would either stall (too high) or look bad forever (too low). ABR turns a freeze into a momentary quality dip, which is exactly the rebuffer-ratio target from the requirements.

Most players start conservatively and ramp up — fast start, then quality — which directly serves the sub-two-second startup target.

<h2 id="cdn-delivery">CDN Delivery</h2>

Delivery is, fundamentally, a **caching** problem. The capacity math said it plainly: 25 Tbps of peak egress is impossible to serve from an origin, so a CDN fans the load out to hundreds of edge points of presence near viewers, and the single most important number in the whole delivery half is the **cache hit ratio**. At 95%, the origin sees only the missed 5% (~1.25 Tbps); nudging the hit ratio higher cuts origin load and cost almost linearly. Everything about delivery is in service of keeping that ratio high.

Two properties make video unusually cache-friendly. First, **segments are immutable**: once `seg_042.ts` is written it never changes, so it can be cached forever with no invalidation worries — the hardest part of caching simply doesn't apply. Second, **popularity is extremely skewed**: a handful of viral titles draw a huge share of views while a long tail is watched rarely. So a small, hot working set lives permanently in edge cache and serves the bulk of traffic, while the cold tail rides occasional cache misses to the origin — and that's fine, because the tail is, by definition, low-volume.

Two more mechanisms tighten it. An **origin shield** — a mid-tier cache between the edges and the origin — collapses misses: when fifty edges all miss the same cold segment, they hit the shield, which fetches from origin **once** and serves the other forty-nine, so the origin never sees a stampede. And for predictable spikes (a big premiere), you **prewarm** the edges by pushing the new title's segments out ahead of demand, so the launch is a cache hit from the first second instead of a thundering herd of misses.

<h2 id="storage-and-cost">Storage & Cost</h2>

Storage is the quiet cost that compounds. Because every upload fans out into a rendition ladder, stored bytes multiply by the rung count, and the platform adds hundreds of terabytes a day that never get smaller — the capacity section's 300 TB/day grows forever. Two levers keep this sane. First, **tiering**: recent and popular content lives on fast, more expensive storage near the CDN, while the cold tail is migrated to cheap archival tiers — rarely watched, rarely fetched, so high retrieval latency is acceptable. Second, **garbage collection**: failed and abandoned transcodes, orphaned segments from re-encodes, and renditions nobody streams are swept up, because at this scale waste is measured in petabytes.

The deeper point is that for streaming, the **egress bill, not the storage bill, usually dominates** — moving bytes to viewers costs more than keeping them. That reframes the CDN and the cache hit ratio as *cost* decisions, not just latency decisions: every percentage point of hit ratio is bytes you don't pay to serve from origin, and every unnecessary rendition is storage *and* potential egress you're paying for. This is why the rendition ladder is a deliberate trade — enough rungs to serve every device and network well, but not so many that you multiply storage and cost for renditions almost nobody picks.

/>

The theme: **the source and the immutable segments are your safety net.** Because the durable source can be re-transcoded and segments are immutable and cached, most failures degrade gracefully — a transcode retries, a cached video keeps playing through an origin blip, a PoP reroutes — rather than losing data or corrupting playback. The two failures to design hardest against are the **incomplete publish** (guarded by atomic, manifest-complete publishing) and the **growing backlog** (guarded by autoscaling and shedding), because those are the ones that silently break the experience.

<h2 id="security-drm">Security & DRM</h2>

A streaming platform serves valuable content over open HTTP, which invites theft of both the content and the bandwidth. Defenses stack along the delivery path.

- **Signed, expiring URLs.** Segment and manifest URLs are signed with a short-lived token tied to the user/session, so a link can't be shared or scraped to pull the whole library — an expired token is rejected at the edge. This is the baseline, and it leans on the same fast token checks as the Rate Limiter's path.
- **DRM for premium content.** For licensed or paid content, segments are **encrypted** and the player must obtain a decryption key from a **license server** that enforces entitlements (who, what device, how long). DRM (Widevine/FairPlay/PlayReady) raises the cost of piracy from "save the file" to "break the crypto," which is the right bar for high-value catalogues.
- **Hotlink protection and geo-blocking.** Edge rules reject requests with the wrong referrer/origin (so other sites can't embed and leech your bandwidth) and enforce geographic licensing (a title available in one region is blocked in another).
- **Abuse and scraping.** Rate-limit manifest and segment requests per token, and watch for patterns that look like bulk ripping rather than human viewing.

<Callout variant="info" title="Tokens at the edge, keys at a license server">
The clean split is that **access** is enforced cheaply and everywhere — signed-URL token checks run at the CDN edge, so an unauthorized request never even reaches the origin — while **decryption** is enforced centrally by a license server that hands out keys only to entitled, authenticated players. Access control keeps bandwidth from being stolen; DRM keeps the bytes themselves useless without a key. Most platforms run signed URLs for everything and add DRM only for the premium tier, because DRM adds real player and operational complexity.
</Callout>

<h2 id="tradeoffs-alternatives">Trade-offs & Alternatives</h2>

Every recommendation here bought something and gave up something. Stating both is the difference between an answer and a strong answer.

<DecisionRecord
  title="The one-sentence design"
  choice="Upload resumable chunks directly to object storage; transcode asynchronously off a queue into a segmented adaptive-bitrate ladder, publishing a video as 'ready' only when its full manifest exists; serve playback as immutable segments through a CDN where cache hit ratio is the dominant lever; and tier storage while protecting content with signed URLs and DRM."
  rationale="The async queue decouples spiky, unbounded uploads from a fixed, autoscaled transcode fleet, so neither blocks the other. Segmenting into a rendition ladder is what enables both parallel transcode and client-driven ABR, which keeps playback smooth by turning network dips into quality dips. Immutable segments plus skewed popularity make delivery a near-perfect caching problem, so a CDN absorbs the 25 Tbps and the origin sees only misses. Keeping the source as the durable record makes every derived artifact safe to lose and regenerate."
  alternatives="Synchronous/inline transcode — simpler but blocks the request for minutes and couples upload to compute; just-in-time transcoding (encode on first request) — saves storage on the cold tail but adds playback latency and origin compute; server-driven bitrate — simpler client but blind to the last mile, so it stalls or under-delivers; serving from origin without a CDN — impossible at this egress; a single CDN — simpler but a single point of failure versus multi-CDN."
  revisitWhen="The product adds live or low-latency streaming (a different ingest pipeline), the catalogue's popularity curve flattens (changing the caching and just-in-time calculus), egress or storage costs dominate enough to retune the rendition ladder, or global residency requirements force multi-region storage."
/>

Three axes capture the real tension:

- **How many renditions.** More rungs serve more devices and networks well but multiply storage, transcode cost, and potential egress; fewer rungs are cheaper but leave some viewers over- or under-served. The ladder is tuned to the audience, not maxed out.
- **Eager vs. just-in-time transcode.** Transcoding every rendition up front gives instant playback at the cost of storing renditions nobody may watch; transcoding on first request saves storage on the cold tail but adds latency to that first view. Most platforms transcode the common rungs eagerly and consider JIT only for the rare ones.
- **HLS vs. DASH (and single vs. multi-CDN).** The streaming protocols are similar in spirit; the real operational lever is CDN strategy — a single CDN is simpler but a single point of failure, while multi-CDN adds steering complexity for redundancy and cost arbitrage.

<h2 id="interview-summary">Interview Summary</h2>

<Callout variant="interview" title="A 60-second end-to-end answer">
"A video streaming platform is really two systems joined at object storage. On ingest, the client uploads resumable chunks straight to object storage, and that upload enqueues an asynchronous transcode job — transcoding is minutes of CPU, so it can never run inline. A worker fan-outs the source into a bitrate ladder, segments each rendition into small immutable chunks, writes a manifest, and marks the video ready only when the whole manifest exists, so playback never dead-ends on a missing segment. On delivery, playback is adaptive bitrate: the player downloads a manifest and picks a rendition per segment based on its measured bandwidth, dropping a rung to avoid a rebuffer and climbing back up — client-driven because only the client sees the last mile. Delivery is fundamentally a CDN/caching problem: peak egress is tens of Tbps, impossible from an origin, so a CDN serves it and the cache hit ratio is the dominant lever — immutable segments and skewed popularity make video almost perfectly cacheable, and an origin shield collapses misses. Storage explodes because one source becomes many renditions, so I tier hot vs cold and garbage-collect failed transcodes, and I protect content with signed URLs at the edge plus DRM for premium. The whole thing is async fan-out on ingest, cache-dominated reads on delivery."
</Callout>
