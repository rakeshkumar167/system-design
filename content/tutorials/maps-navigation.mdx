<h2 id="interview-framing">Interview Framing</h2>

A maps and navigation service answers a deceptively simple question — "how do I drive from here to there, and how long will it take?" — over a road network the size of a continent, for billions of requests a day, while the answer keeps changing because traffic does. Google Maps, Apple Maps, Waze, and every routing API underneath a ride-hailing or delivery app are this system. From the outside it looks like a textbook exercise: model the roads as a graph, run Dijkstra, return the shortest path. That framing gets you a correct router that works beautifully on a city and then falls over the instant the graph is a country, because Dijkstra on a 50-million-node graph settles tens of millions of nodes per query and you have tens of thousands of queries per second.

The hard part is **answering shortest-path queries fast, at scale, over a graph whose edge weights never stop moving**. Three problems fall out of that. First, **the query is too expensive to compute from scratch**: Dijkstra and `A*` are correct but settle a huge fraction of a continental graph per route, which is hopeless at the required latency and QPS — so the dominant idea is **precomputation**, building an offline **hierarchy** of the road network (contraction hierarchies, or customizable route planning) so that an online query is a bidirectional search touching a few hundred nodes and returns in milliseconds. Second, **the graph is alive**: edge weights are *travel times* that change minute to minute with congestion, so the system ingests a firehose of **GPS probes**, fuses them into live weights, and must fold those new weights into the precomputed hierarchy *without* rebuilding it from scratch. Third, the **inputs and the map itself are messy**: raw GPS is noisy and addresses are human, so you need **map matching** and **geocoding** to get onto the graph at all, and the map imagery is served as **tiles** that outnumber routes ~50× but are static and cacheable. The reframe to lead with: *it looks like running Dijkstra on the road graph, but Dijkstra settles half a continent per query and the weights change with traffic — so the real design is precomputing a hierarchy of the network so a route is a millisecond in-memory lookup, continuously re-weighting that graph from GPS probes, and serving the map itself as cacheable tiles from the edge.*

Spend the opening minutes scoping. Good questions to ask:

- Driving routes only, or multi-modal (transit, walking, cycling)? Each mode is a different graph and cost model; driving with live traffic is the rich core.
- Does the route need to reflect *live* traffic, or is free-flow / historical good enough? Live traffic is what forces the dynamic-weights machinery.
- How fresh must traffic be — seconds, or a couple of minutes? This sizes the probe pipeline and how often you re-customize the hierarchy.
- Do we serve the map tiles too, or just the routing API? Tiles are a separate, CDN-dominated subsystem.
- What scale and coverage — a metro, a country, the planet? Scale is what kills the naive Dijkstra framing and motivates precomputation.

Three targets shape every later decision. First, **low route latency at high QPS**: a navigation request must return in tens of milliseconds, and there are tens of thousands per second — which is exactly the combination plain Dijkstra cannot meet, and the reason precomputation is the spine of the design. Second, **read-heavy scale with a cacheable layer**: the system is overwhelmingly reads (routes and tiles), and tiles in particular are static and can be pushed to a CDN, so the architecture splits the **compute-bound route engine** from the **cache-bound tile delivery**. Third, **traffic freshness with relaxed consistency**: weights should track reality within minutes, but a route need not reflect a jam that started one second ago — **eventual consistency** of traffic is acceptable, which is what lets the system aggregate probes and re-customize the hierarchy on an interval rather than per-event. Note what's *relaxed*: routing can **degrade gracefully** to free-flow or historical weights if the live traffic feed is unavailable, because a slightly-stale route beats no route. The system trades perfect, instantaneous traffic accuracy for speed, scale, and availability.

The takeaways. First, **route QPS is modest but naive Dijkstra is impossible**: a billion routes a day is only ~**11,600/sec** on average — a rate any service tier handles — but a plain Dijkstra settles ~half the graph, ~**25 million** nodes, *per query*, which across the fleet is ~**289 billion** node-settles per second. That number is not "expensive," it's physically impossible, and it is the single fact that forces the whole design offline. Second, **precomputation collapses the per-query cost ~62,500×**: a contraction hierarchy answers a query by settling a few hundred nodes instead of 25 million, turning routing into a **millisecond in-memory lookup** — the difference between impossible and trivial. Third, **the routable graph fits in RAM**: ~**3.84 GB** of edges means the entire road network lives in memory on each route-engine replica, so routing is served from RAM by **stateless replicas**, never a database query per request. Fourth, **tiles dwarf routes but are cacheable**: ~**579,000 tiles/sec** (~50× the route rate) sounds frightening until you remember tiles are static, immutable per map version, and served from a **CDN edge** — a delivery problem the edge absorbs, not a compute one. The capacity story: maps is hard because of the *per-query graph search*, which precomputation moves offline, not because of raw request volume.

<h2 id="entity-model">Entity Model</h2>

The most important idea is that the edge weight is **travel time**, not distance, and that there are *two* weights per edge — a static free-flow time and a live, traffic-adjusted time — because that duality is what the routing and traffic subsystems are built around. Everything else (the hierarchy, tiles, geocoding index) is derived from or supports this graph.

Four supporting ideas complete the picture. A **Node** is an intersection — an id plus a coordinate — and the graph is stored as **adjacency lists** so expanding a node's neighbors is cheap. **Turn restrictions** ("no left turn here") mean the cost of traversing a segment can depend on where you came from, which is modeled either with turn costs or by expanding intersections into a small per-turn subgraph. The **contraction hierarchy** is the derived acceleration structure: a node ordering plus **shortcut** edges that preserve shortest-path distances, built offline and re-customized when weights change. And the **tiles** and **geocoding index** are read-only projections — the map you see and the address-to-coordinate lookup — that ride alongside the routable graph rather than being part of it. The discipline: one authoritative weighted graph, two weights per edge (static and live), and a derived hierarchy that makes it fast.

<h2 id="high-level-architecture">High-Level Architecture</h2>

The architecture separates the three jobs the API implies: turning messy input into graph positions (map matching / geocoding), searching the precomputed graph fast (the route engine), and keeping that graph's weights current (the traffic pipeline) — with tile serving as a fourth, CDN-dominated path off to the side.

<MapsNavigationArchitecture />

- **Client.** A phone or car that asks for routes and renders the map from tiles.
- **Routing Gateway.** The API edge: authenticates, rate-limits, and fans a request out to map matching and the route engine.
- **Map Matcher / Geocoder.** Snaps raw coordinates (and resolves addresses) onto the nearest routable graph nodes — routing can't start until the endpoints are *on* the graph.
- **Route Engine.** The compute core: it holds the **precomputed contraction hierarchy in memory** and answers a query with a bidirectional "upward" search settling a few hundred nodes. Stateless and horizontally replicated — each replica loads the same graph from the store.
- **Graph / CH Store.** The durable road network plus the precomputed hierarchy (node ordering + shortcut edges). Built offline; loaded into every route-engine replica's RAM.
- **Traffic Pipeline.** Ingests the firehose of **GPS probes**, aggregates them per segment, and fuses them with historical/predictive patterns into **live edge weights**.
- **Live Weights store.** The current traffic-adjusted time for each segment, applied to the hierarchy via cheap **CRP metric customization** so routes reflect current conditions.
- **Tile Service + CDN.** Pre-rendered map tiles served from the **CDN edge**; only cache misses reach the origin tile service.

The decisive boundary is **precompute the graph offline and serve routing from an in-memory hierarchy, while traffic flows in as a separate stream that only re-weights it**. The Graph/CH store and the offline preprocessing make queries fast; the in-memory route engine makes them low-latency and horizontally scalable; the traffic pipeline keeps the weights honest without touching the graph's structure; and the CDN turns the high-volume tile traffic into someone else's caching problem. Every section that follows elaborates one piece: how the graph is modeled (road graph), why the naive search is too slow (shortest path), how precomputation fixes it (the centerpiece), how messy input gets onto the graph (map matching), how the weights stay current (traffic & ETA), and how the map itself is delivered (tiles).

<h2 id="detailed-flows">Detailed Flows</h2>

Four flows cover the system: answering a route query, map-matching a GPS trace onto the graph, ingesting traffic to re-weight the graph, and fetching a map tile. The first is the latency-critical hot path; the others keep it fed and rendered.

**Answer a route query.** The client asks for a route between two coordinates; the matcher snaps origin and destination to the nearest graph nodes; the route engine runs a bidirectional upward search over the in-memory hierarchy, reading shortcut edges, and returns the path plus ETA. This is the millisecond hot path precomputation exists to enable.

**Map-match a GPS trace.** Raw GPS is noisy — points scatter tens of meters off the road and can appear on the wrong street. The matcher pulls candidate nearby segments and runs a hidden Markov model that balances each point's GPS proximity against whether consecutive snapped points form a *plausible* drive, returning the most likely road path.

**Ingest traffic and re-weight.** Devices stream GPS probes; the traffic pipeline aggregates them per segment and fuses them with historical patterns into updated live edge times; the route engine folds those new weights into the hierarchy via cheap **CRP metric customization**, so subsequent routes reflect current traffic without rebuilding the graph's structure.

<h2 id="road-graph">The Road Network as a Graph</h2>

The foundation is modeling roads as a **directed, weighted graph**: **nodes** are intersections, **edges** are road segments, and an edge's **weight is travel time**. Three modeling choices matter. The graph must be **directed**, because one-way streets and asymmetric travel times (uphill vs downhill, a ramp on vs off a highway) mean an edge from A to B is not the same as B to A. The weight must be **time, not distance**, because "fastest" is what users want and a longer highway routinely beats a shorter surface street — distance is just one input to time, alongside speed limit and congestion. And **turn restrictions** ("no left turn," "no U-turn") make cost depend on the *pair* of segments you traverse, not a single edge, which is handled either by attaching **turn costs** or by expanding each intersection into a small subgraph of per-movement nodes.

The scale is what makes this interesting. A continental road network is tens of millions of nodes and over a hundred million directed edges, but — as the capacity model showed — that's only a few gigabytes when stored as compact adjacency, so the **entire graph fits in RAM**. That single fact shapes the architecture: routing is a CPU-and-memory problem solved by **stateless route-engine replicas each holding the whole graph**, not a database traversal that fetches neighbors over the network per hop (which would add a network round-trip to every one of the millions of node expansions a search performs). The graph is built offline from raw map data (OpenStreetMap, commercial map providers, municipal feeds), versioned, and shipped to the replicas. Get the model right — directed, time-weighted, turn-aware, in memory — and everything downstream is "search this graph quickly."

<h2 id="shortest-path">Shortest-Path Routing</h2>

The classic answer to "shortest path in a weighted graph" is **Dijkstra's algorithm**: settle nodes in increasing order of distance from the source, relaxing each neighbor, until you reach the destination. It's correct and optimal, but it explores **outward in all directions** like a growing circle, so to reach a destination far across the graph it settles a huge fraction of all nodes in between — the capacity model's ~25 million per query. **`A*`** improves on this by adding a **heuristic**: it prioritizes nodes by distance-so-far *plus* an estimate of distance-remaining (the straight-line "as the crow flies" distance to the goal, which never overestimates and so keeps `A*` optimal). The heuristic pulls the search *toward* the goal, settling fewer nodes — but on a continental graph it's still far too many, because the straight-line estimate is weak around real road structure (mountains, water, limited-access highways). **Bidirectional search** — running two searches, one forward from the source and one backward from the target, until they meet — roughly halves the explored area, but "half of impossible" is still impossible.

The key realization is structural, and it's the hinge of the entire design: **queries are numerous and the graph is (mostly) static**. You answer billions of routes against a road network that changes its *structure* rarely (new roads are infrequent) and its *weights* only on a slow cadence (traffic, every few minutes). That asymmetry is an invitation to **move work from query time to preprocessing time**: do expensive computation *once*, offline, over the static graph, to build a structure that makes every subsequent query cheap. Plain Dijkstra and `A*` do zero preprocessing and pay the full cost on every query; the scalable approach inverts that. This is the setup for precomputation — the recognition that the right place to spend CPU is *before* the queries arrive, not during each one.

<h2 id="precomputation">Precomputation & Hierarchies</h2>

This is the centerpiece. **Precomputation** trades expensive offline work for cheap online queries, and the dominant technique is **Contraction Hierarchies (CH)**. The idea: order all nodes by "importance" (roughly, how central they are to long routes — a highway interchange is important, a cul-de-sac is not), then **contract** nodes one at a time from least to most important. Contracting a node removes it and, wherever removing it would have destroyed a shortest path through it, adds a **shortcut edge** between its neighbors that preserves the exact distance. The result is the original graph plus a layer of shortcuts that summarize long stretches of road. A **query** then runs a bidirectional search that only ever moves *upward* in the importance order — forward from the source going up, backward from the target going up — and the two meet at a high-importance node. Because the search only climbs, it ignores the vast low-level detail and settles a few hundred nodes instead of millions: the ~**62,500×** reduction from the capacity model. Long-distance routes naturally ride the shortcuts (which correspond to highways), exactly as a human reads a map.

The catch is **traffic**: CH bakes the edge weights into the shortcuts during contraction, so when weights change you'd have to re-contract — too slow to do every few minutes. **Customizable Route Planning (CRP)** solves this by **splitting preprocessing into two phases**. The first, **metric-independent** phase partitions the graph into a hierarchy of cells based only on its *structure* (which is stable) and is done rarely. The second, **metric customization** phase computes the shortcut weights for the *current* metric (the live travel times) and is **cheap and fast** — seconds, not hours — so it can re-run every time traffic updates. This separation is the key to a live system: you pay the heavy structural preprocessing once, then absorb constantly-changing traffic with a quick re-customization, never rebuilding the partition. The trade-off is explicit and worth stating: **heavy offline preprocessing (and ongoing customization) in exchange for millisecond queries**. You spend CPU and memory ahead of time, and a small amount continuously, so that the billions of latency-critical queries are nearly free — which is the only way the numbers close.

<h2 id="map-matching">Map Matching & Geocoding</h2>

Routing operates on graph nodes, but the inputs to the system are not graph nodes — they're **noisy GPS coordinates** and **human addresses**. Two preprocessing steps bridge the gap. **Geocoding** turns an address or place name ("1600 Amphitheatre Pkwy") into a coordinate, and **reverse geocoding** does the opposite; both are a **geospatial + text-search** problem solved with a spatial index (a quadtree, geohash, or S2 cells) over places and address ranges, distinct from the routing graph. Once you have a coordinate, you still must get *onto* the graph by **snapping** it to the nearest routable segment — a GPS fix in a parking lot or on the wrong side of a divided highway has to be attached to the right edge before a search can begin.

The hard version of snapping is **map matching**: reconciling a *sequence* of jittery GPS points (a driver's trace) onto the most plausible *path* through the road network. A naive "snap each point to its nearest road" fails badly — consecutive points can snap to different parallel streets, producing a physically impossible zig-zag. The standard solution is a **hidden Markov model**: each GPS point has several candidate nearby segments (emission probabilities favor closer roads), and transitions between consecutive candidates are scored by whether the road network actually connects them with a drive whose length matches the GPS movement (transition probabilities favor plausible motion). The most likely sequence — found with the Viterbi algorithm — is the matched path, robustly threading noisy points onto real roads. Map matching is what makes live traffic possible at all: it's how raw probe traces become "this car traversed *these* segments at *these* times," which is the input the traffic pipeline aggregates.

<h2 id="traffic-eta">Live Traffic & ETA</h2>

What makes maps *alive* is that edge weights are travel times that change with congestion, and the system must track them. The raw signal is a **firehose of GPS probes** — anonymized location pings from millions of devices running navigation. The pipeline **map-matches** each trace onto segments (previous section), then **aggregates** per segment over a recent window to estimate current speed, and **fuses** that with **historical and predictive** models: traffic is highly periodic (rush hour every weekday, lighter on holidays), so a segment with few live probes right now can fall back on its typical speed for this time-of-day and day-of-week. The fused result is the **live travel time** per segment, which flows into the **CRP metric customization** so the hierarchy reflects current conditions — and because this is a re-customization, not a rebuild, it completes fast enough to run continuously.

**ETA** is more subtle than summing live weights along the route, because of a time dimension: a long trip might take an hour, so the congestion on a segment you'll reach in 50 minutes is not today's *current* congestion but its *predicted* congestion at your arrival time. A good ETA therefore integrates **predicted** speeds along the route as a function of *when you'll get to each segment*, not a snapshot at departure. This is where historical/predictive models earn their keep: they let the ETA anticipate the rush hour you're driving into or out of. Two consistency notes make the design tractable. First, traffic is **eventually consistent** — a route computed against weights that are a minute or two old is fine, which is what lets the pipeline batch and aggregate rather than react to every probe. Second, the system must **degrade gracefully**: if the live feed stalls, routing falls back to historical or free-flow weights and keeps working, because a slightly-stale ETA is far better than an outage. Reliability of *some* answer beats freshness of a perfect one.

<h2 id="tile-serving">Map Tile Serving</h2>

The map you look at is delivered as **tiles**: the world is rendered into a **pyramid** of square images addressed by **zoom level and x/y grid coordinates**, where each zoom level quadruples the tile count (zoom *z* splits each tile into four at *z+1*). A client viewing an area fetches the handful of tiles covering the viewport at the current zoom, which is why — per the capacity model — tiles outnumber route requests ~50× yet are individually trivial. Tiles come in two flavors: **raster** (pre-rendered PNG/WebP images, simple to serve but heavy and fixed-style) and **vector** (compact geometry the client renders, enabling smooth zoom, rotation, and restyling), with vector tiles now dominant for their flexibility and smaller size.

The decisive property is that tiles are **static and immutable per map version**: the tile for `z/x/y` doesn't change until the underlying map data is republished, so it can carry a long cache lifetime and an immutable marker. That turns tile serving into a **caching and delivery problem, not a compute problem** — the polar opposite of the route engine. Tiles are pre-rendered offline (or rendered once on first request and cached), pushed to a **CDN**, and served from the **edge** close to users; only a cache miss reaches the origin tile service, so origin load is a trickle and the user gets fast, local delivery. This reuses exactly the CDN intuition from the Pastebin and Video Streaming designs: immutable, addressable content fronted by an edge cache. When the map is republished, you bump a **version** in the tile path (or purge), so caches naturally fetch the new generation without serving stale geometry. The contrast is the lesson: one map service contains a compute-bound, in-memory graph search *and* a cache-bound static-delivery pipeline, and they scale by completely different means.

<h2 id="scalability-evolution">Scalability & Evolution</h2>

Don't build the globally-partitioned, CRP-customized system on day one. Each stage answers a specific pressure and adds a specific cost — name the trigger before taking on the complexity.

<TradeoffTable
  columns={["Stage", "Trigger", "Cost"]}
  rows={[
    ["Single-server Dijkstra", "A city-sized graph, low QPS", "Settles too much of the graph per query; falls over as the map and traffic grow"],
    ["A* + in-memory graph", "Bigger graph, more queries", "Goal-directed search helps, but still settles far too many nodes at continental scale"],
    ["Precomputed CH/CRP + replicated in-memory engines", "Continental graph, high QPS, live traffic", "Offline preprocessing + ongoing metric customization; an in-memory graph to load and version"],
    ["Global multi-region + regional graph partitions + edge tile CDN", "Worldwide coverage and locality", "Cross-region/partition routing for long trips; partition ownership; more operational surface"],
  ]}
  recommendedRow={2}
/>

The progression tracks the pressure you're buying out of. A single-server Dijkstra is fine for a city and collapses as the graph and query rate grow. Adding `A*` and keeping the graph in memory buys headroom but doesn't change the fundamental "settles too much" problem. The center of gravity for any real system is **precomputed contraction hierarchies / CRP served from replicated in-memory route engines**, with live traffic folded in by metric customization — this is where queries become millisecond-cheap, the fleet scales horizontally (stateless replicas), and traffic stays current. Only planetary scale needs **multi-region deployment with regional graph partitions and edge tile CDNs**, which pays for locality and coverage with genuinely hard cross-partition routing (a trip spanning two regions must stitch partitions together). Most maps systems are well-served by "precomputed hierarchy, in-memory replicas, CRP customization for traffic, CDN for tiles."

Reason about every dependency in the same matrix — blast radius, detection, mitigation, recovery. A maps system's guiding rule is **always return a usable answer, even a slightly stale one**: a route on historical weights beats no route, and a cached tile beats a blank map.

The theme: **everything degrades to a graceful fallback rather than an outage.** A dead route engine is just a stateless replica swap; a stalled traffic feed falls back to historical weights; a bad graph build is rolled back by version; poor GPS degrades to nearest-segment snapping; a tile CDN hiccup serves stale-while-revalidate from the edge; a probe spike sheds load while routing continues on last-good weights; and a region outage fails over to another region. Because the routable graph is precomputed, versioned, and held in memory, recovery is almost always "reload the last good graph and resume," and because traffic is a separate, eventually-consistent stream, losing it briefly costs freshness, not availability. The one outcome the design refuses is a hard failure that returns nothing — there is almost always a slightly-staler answer to fall back to.

<h2 id="tradeoffs-alternatives">Trade-offs & Alternatives</h2>

Every recommendation here bought something and gave up something. Stating both is the difference between an answer and a strong answer.

<DecisionRecord
  title="The one-sentence design"
  choice="Model the roads as a directed, time-weighted graph; precompute a contraction hierarchy (with a CRP-style split of metric-independent preprocessing from cheap metric customization) and serve routing from stateless, in-memory route-engine replicas that answer a bidirectional upward search in milliseconds; keep weights current with a GPS-probe traffic pipeline that map-matches, aggregates, and re-customizes the hierarchy; map-match and geocode inputs onto the graph; and serve the map itself as static z/x/y tiles from a CDN."
  rationale="Naive Dijkstra/A* settle a large fraction of a continental graph per query and are impossible at the required QPS, so the keystone is precomputation: move the expensive work offline into a hierarchy so each online query is a few-hundred-node lookup. CRP's two-phase split is what makes that compatible with live traffic — heavy structural preprocessing once, cheap customization continuously. The graph fits in RAM, so stateless in-memory replicas give horizontal scale and low latency. Tiles are static and cacheable, so a CDN absorbs the highest-volume traffic. Eventual consistency and graceful degradation keep the system answering through failures."
  alternatives="Plain Dijkstra/A* per query — correct but impossibly slow at scale; precomputing all-pairs shortest paths — infeasible storage at tens of millions of nodes; pure contraction hierarchies without CRP — fast queries but weights baked into shortcuts, so re-contracting for every traffic update is too slow; raster-only tiles — simpler but heavier and fixed-style versus flexible vector tiles; a single global graph — simpler than regional partitions but doesn't localize or scale to planetary coverage; reacting to every probe synchronously — needless load versus aggregating with eventual consistency."
  revisitWhen="Multi-modal routing (transit/walking/cycling) is required (different graphs and cost models), sub-minute traffic freshness is demanded (tighter customization cadence and probe pipeline), planetary scale forces regional graph partitions with cross-partition stitching, or routes must satisfy complex constraints (truck restrictions, time-windows) that strain the hierarchy's assumptions."
/>

Four axes capture the real tension:

- **Naive search vs precomputed hierarchies.** Dijkstra/`A*` need no preprocessing but settle too much per query; CH/CRP pay heavy offline (and ongoing customization) cost to make queries millisecond-cheap. At scale, precomputation always wins — the only question is CH vs CRP.
- **CH vs CRP.** Pure contraction hierarchies give the fastest static queries but bake weights into shortcuts, so live traffic forces slow re-contraction; CRP separates structure from metric so traffic is absorbed by fast customization. Choose CRP when weights change often.
- **Freshness vs query cost.** Fresher traffic means more frequent (costlier) customization and a heavier probe pipeline; eventual consistency on the order of minutes is the sweet spot for driving.
- **Vector vs raster tiles.** Raster is dead-simple to serve but heavy and fixed-style; vector tiles are compact and client-rendered (smooth zoom/rotate/restyle) at the cost of client complexity. Vector is the modern default; both ride a CDN.
