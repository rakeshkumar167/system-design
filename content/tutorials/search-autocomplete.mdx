<h2 id="interview-framing">Interview Framing</h2>

Search autocomplete — *typeahead* — is the dropdown of suggested queries that appears the instant you start typing into a search box. Type "ne" and it offers "netflix", "news", "nearby restaurants"; each additional keystroke refines the list. Google, Bing, Amazon, YouTube, and every app with a search bar runs this system. From the outside it's a textbook prefix lookup: hold all known queries in a **trie** (a prefix tree), walk down the characters the user typed, and return everything underneath. That framing produces something that works on a dictionary of a thousand words and then falls apart in two ways at real scale — it returns *millions* of completions for a one-letter prefix with no idea which ten matter, and it tries to compute that answer fresh on every keystroke of a million-QPS firehose.

The hard part is **ranking the suggestions and serving them instantly, per keystroke, at enormous read volume**. Three things dominate, and prefix matching is the easy one. First, **ranking**: "a" prefixes millions of queries, you have ~10 slots and a sub-100 ms budget, so the design isn't "find the matches" but "have the **top-k best completions already computed** at every node of the trie" — a query becomes an O(prefix-length) walk plus reading a tiny cached list, never a scan of the subtree. Second, **read amplification**: because the suggestions update on *every character typed*, autocomplete QPS is a large multiple of the actual search QPS — over a million requests per second — so nothing can be computed per query; the ranked index must live **in memory, replicated, and cached**. Third, **building and refreshing that ranked index**: the popularity scores come from aggregating **billions of past search-query logs** in a batch pipeline that rebuilds the trie periodically, plus a **real-time layer** that injects **trending** queries the hours-old batch misses — and a **safety** filter that keeps harmful completions out. The reframe to lead with: *it looks like a trie walk, but the trie is the easy part — the design is precomputed top-k at every node (ranking, not matching), a read-amplified in-memory serving tier absorbing a million-plus QPS, a query-log aggregation pipeline that builds the ranked index, a trending layer for freshness, and safety filtering. Matching a prefix is trivial; choosing and serving the best ten completions instantly, per keystroke, at scale, is the design.*

Spend the opening minutes scoping. Good questions to ask:

- What are we completing — full past search queries, or product names / entities? It shapes where the candidate phrases come from.
- How many suggestions, and how fast must they appear? (Usually ~5–10, within a few tens of milliseconds so the dropdown keeps up with typing.)
- How fresh must suggestions be — is same-day popularity fine, or must breaking/trending queries show within minutes? Freshness drives whether you need a real-time layer.
- Personalized or global? Personalization adds per-user signals on top of global popularity.
- Do we need typo tolerance, and multiple languages/regions? Both widen the matching problem.

Three targets shape every later decision. First, **latency is the whole point**: the dropdown has to keep up with a person typing, so each per-keystroke response must come back in low tens of milliseconds — which rules out computing anything expensive at query time and forces **precomputed top-k served from memory**. Second, **the load is overwhelmingly read and amplified**: every keystroke is a request, so the system runs at a large multiple of the search rate (the capacity section puts it at ~1M+ QPS), which means the ranked index must be **small, in-memory, replicated, and cached**, not queried out of a database per request. Third, **freshness with safety**: popularity data is aggregated in a batch that's hours stale, so a **real-time layer** must surface trending queries promptly, while a **filter** keeps harmful, offensive, or manipulated completions out — because autocomplete *publishes* suggestions to users and that carries responsibility. Note what's *relaxed*: the index is **eventually consistent** (a suggestion appearing minutes late is fine), and autocomplete is **best-effort and optional** — if it fails, the user must still be able to type and submit their search. The system trades perfect freshness and completeness for instant, ranked, safe suggestions at massive read scale.

The takeaways. Second, **the ranked index is tiny relative to that load**: 500 million indexed phrases at ~40 bytes each is only ~**20 GB**, small enough to **replicate in full into the RAM of every serving node** — so reads scale by simply adding identical stateless replicas (no fan-out, no cross-node coordination to answer one prefix), and ~**116 nodes** carry the QPS. Contrast this with the **Web Crawler**'s multi-petabyte corpus: autocomplete deliberately indexes only the *popular* phrases, keeping the structure that must be fast small enough to live in memory. Third, **the ranking data is a firehose**: ~**1 TB/day** of query-log events feed the batch pipeline that computes popularity. The capacity story: autocomplete is hard because of the **read amplification and ranking**, not because storing prefixes is difficult — the index fits in RAM precisely so a million-plus QPS can be served in milliseconds.

Two supporting entities complete the picture. The discipline: the trie is a **derived, rebuildable read-optimized structure** with top-k cached per node; the source of truth for popularity is the append-only QueryEvent log.

<h2 id="high-level-architecture">High-Level Architecture</h2>

The architecture is two planes that meet at the index: a **read plane** that answers prefixes from an in-memory trie in milliseconds, and a **build plane** that aggregates query logs into that trie offline — with a real-time layer bridging the freshness gap.

<SearchAutocompleteArchitecture />

- **Client.** The search box; debounces keystrokes and calls `/autocomplete` as the user types.
- **Autocomplete Service.** Stateless; walks the in-memory trie to the prefix node and returns its precomputed top-k. Sized to the read load; each replica holds a full index copy.
- **Suggestion Cache.** A cache (often at the edge/CDN) in front of the service that absorbs the hottest prefixes, so most keystrokes never touch a serving node.
- **Trie Index.** The ranked structure held **in memory** on every serving node — a trie with the top-k cached at each node. Small (~20 GB), replicated, swapped atomically when a new version is built.
- **Query Log.** The append-only firehose of search events (`/events`), the raw popularity signal.
- **Aggregator.** A batch job that consumes the log and counts per-phrase frequencies over a window.
- **Index Builder.** Turns aggregated, decayed, filtered scores into a new trie with fresh top-k lists and publishes it as an immutable version.
- **Trending.** A real-time layer that watches recent events for velocity spikes and injects hot phrases into the served top-k between batch rebuilds.

The decisive boundary is **the read plane serves precomputed answers from memory, and the build plane computes those answers offline from logs** — the two are deliberately decoupled by the immutable index. The trie's per-node top-k makes a read O(prefix length); the small index size lets every node hold a full replica so reads scale horizontally; the cache absorbs the hottest prefixes; the batch pipeline does all the heavy ranking where latency doesn't matter; and the trending layer patches the freshness gap the batch leaves. Every section that follows elaborates one piece: how the trie + top-k works, how ranking and safety score the suggestions, how the pipeline builds the index, how the serving tier hits its latency budget, how trending keeps it fresh, and how typo tolerance and personalization refine matches.

<h2 id="trie-topk">The Trie & Precomputed Top-K</h2>

The trie is the centerpiece, and the key insight is that **the trie alone solves matching, not ranking**. A trie (prefix tree) stores phrases by their characters: the path "n" → "e" → "t" leads to every phrase starting "net", and finding the node for a prefix is an O(prefix-length) walk — fast and simple. The trouble is *which* completions to return. The prefix "a" has millions of descendants; the naive approach is to traverse the entire subtree under the prefix node, collect every phrase, sort by score, and take the top ten — which is enormous work for short prefixes and blows the latency budget instantly. You cannot do a subtree scan on the hot path at a million QPS.

The fix is to **precompute and store the top-k completions at every node**. At index-build time, each node is annotated with the k highest-scoring phrases that pass through it (computed bottom-up: a node's top-k is the merge of its children's top-k lists plus any phrase ending there). Then a query is just: walk to the prefix node, **read its cached top-k list, done** — constant work regardless of how many descendants the prefix has. This trades **memory for latency**: storing k entries at every node enlarges the structure (k is small, ~5–10, so it's bounded), and the top-k is only as fresh as the last build (which the trending layer compensates for). Memory can be reined in with a **compressed trie** — a radix/PATRICIA tree that collapses single-child chains, a ternary search tree, or a finite-state transducer (FST) that shares suffixes — but the central idea is unchanged. The principle: a trie makes *matching* a prefix cheap, but serving the *best* completions cheaply requires precomputing the top-k at every node so a read is a walk plus a list read, never a scan — paying memory and a rebuild cadence to buy a constant-time hot path.

<h2 id="ranking-scoring">Ranking, Filtering & Safety</h2>

A suggestion's **score** is what decides the top-k, and getting it right is what separates a useful dropdown from a random one. The dominant signal is **popularity**: how often the phrase has been searched, aggregated from the query logs into a `frequency`. But raw frequency alone is stale and gameable, so it's blended with **recency / time-decay** — recent searches count more, and last year's hits fade — so the suggestions track what people are searching *now*, not cumulatively forever. On top of those global signals, optional **context and personalization** (covered later) can re-rank: the user's language, location, recent queries, or the current app surface. The score is computed offline in the pipeline and baked into the top-k lists; the hot path never recomputes it.

Two guardrails ride on the same scoring stage. The first is **safety and filtering**: because autocomplete *publishes* completions to users — effectively putting words in the search box — it carries responsibility not to suggest harmful, offensive, defamatory, or unsafe phrases. A **blocklist plus classifiers** removes such phrases, applied both at **build time** (they never enter the trie) and at **query time** (a live filter so a takedown is immediate, not delayed until the next rebuild). The second is **demotion of manipulation**: spammy or artificially inflated phrases (bots hammering a query to make it suggest) are detected and down-weighted, so popularity can't be trivially gamed. The principle: rank by **popularity blended with recency decay**, computed offline and cached as the top-k; and treat **filtering/safety as a first-class, two-stage control** (build-time exclusion plus a live query-time filter), because publishing a suggestion is an editorial act, not a neutral lookup.

<h2 id="data-pipeline">Building the Index from Query Logs</h2>

The ranking data has to come from somewhere, and that somewhere is the **query log**: every submitted search is logged as a **QueryEvent**, producing a firehose of ~1 TB/day. A **batch pipeline** (MapReduce/Spark-style) consumes this log and does all the heavy lifting where latency doesn't matter: it **aggregates** events into per-phrase frequencies over a window, applies **time-decay** so recent activity weighs more, runs the **safety filter** to drop blocklisted phrases, computes the **top-k for every trie node** bottom-up, and **publishes the result as a new immutable index version**. Serving nodes then load that version and swap to it **atomically** — an instant cutover from the old trie to the new one, with no half-built state ever served. Because the index is immutable and versioned, a bad build can simply be rolled back to the prior version, and every replica converges on the same data.

This is a **Lambda-architecture** split: a slow, comprehensive **batch layer** that rebuilds the whole ranked index every few hours, plus a fast **real-time layer** (next section) that patches the freshness gap in between. The batch layer's cadence is a deliberate trade — rebuild more often for fresher popularity at higher compute cost, less often to save cost at the price of staleness. The rebuild is itself a **time-triggered job**, the same shape as the **Distributed Job Scheduler** firing a periodic task, and the log it consumes is the same kind of append-only firehose as the **Distributed Logging** platform. The principle: do all the expensive ranking **offline** over the aggregated query log, publish the index as **immutable, atomically-swapped versions** (so serving is consistent and rollback is trivial), and accept a batch cadence for the bulk of freshness while a real-time layer covers the rest.

<h2 id="serving-layer">Serving, Sharding & the Latency Budget</h2>

Serving ~1M+ QPS in single-digit milliseconds works because of one fact established in the capacity section: **the index is small enough to live in RAM**. At ~20 GB, the entire ranked trie fits in the memory of each serving node, so every node holds a **full replica** — and that changes how scaling works. Answering a single prefix needs no fan-out, no scatter-gather, no cross-node coordination: any node can answer any query from its own memory, so you scale reads by simply **adding more identical stateless replicas** behind a load balancer. This is the easy, embarrassingly-parallel kind of scaling, and it's only possible because the structure that must be fast is deliberately kept small (only popular phrases, not the whole corpus). In front of the replicas, a **suggestion cache** (often at the CDN/edge) absorbs the hottest prefixes — a small number of common prefixes account for a huge share of traffic — so most keystrokes are answered without even reaching a serving node.

The **latency budget** is unforgiving because the dropdown must keep up with typing, and two techniques protect it. First, the **client debounces**: rather than firing a request on every single keystroke, it waits a few tens of milliseconds for the typing to settle, collapsing a burst of keystrokes into one request and cutting load substantially. Second, the service treats latency as a hard deadline — it returns the precomputed top-k within the budget or returns **nothing** rather than blocking, since a missing dropdown is far better than a janky, lagging one. When the index eventually outgrows a single node's memory (many languages, entities, personalization), you **shard by prefix** — the first character(s) route to different shards — so each node holds a slice; but for most designs, full replication is simpler and faster. The principle: keep the index small enough to **replicate into RAM** so reads scale by adding stateless replicas, put a **cache** in front for the hot prefixes, **debounce** on the client, and enforce a hard latency deadline (return nothing rather than be slow) — sharding by prefix only when the index no longer fits in one node.

<h2 id="freshness-trending">Freshness & Trending Queries</h2>

The batch-built index is **hours stale**, and that's exactly when it hurts most: a breaking news event, a just-released product, a viral moment — people start searching for it *now*, but the phrase barely existed at the last rebuild, so its batch-computed popularity is near zero and it never makes the top-k. Waiting for the next batch means the dropdown is useless for precisely the queries spiking in real time. The fix is a **real-time layer** that consumes the recent event stream, detects **velocity spikes** — phrases whose *rate of increase* is climbing sharply, not just their raw count — and **injects** those trending phrases into the served top-k between batch rebuilds. This is the fast half of the **Lambda architecture**: the batch layer owns comprehensive, decayed popularity; the real-time layer owns the last few minutes the batch can't see yet.

The important design point is that trending keys on **velocity, not volume**. A perennially popular query like "weather" has huge raw counts every minute but isn't *trending* — it's already in the batch top-k. What you want to catch is the phrase that went from near-zero to a flood in minutes, which signals something *new*. That's also where the risk lives: reacting to spikes invites **manipulation and noise** (a coordinated burst to force a suggestion, or a transient blip that isn't real), so the trending layer pairs velocity thresholds with the same **safety filtering** and spam-demotion as the batch path, and the injection is bounded and decays quickly. The trade-off is **freshness versus stability**: react faster to surface real trends sooner, but risk surfacing noise or manipulated phrases. The principle: bridge the batch index's staleness with a real-time layer that detects **rate-of-change spikes** (not raw popularity) and injects trending phrases into the top-k, while filtering for safety and manipulation so the fast path doesn't become an attack surface.

<h2 id="fuzzy-personalization">Typo Tolerance & Personalization</h2>

Two refinements make the matching both more forgiving and more relevant. The first is **typo tolerance**: people mistype, and an exact trie walk on "netflox" simply falls off the tree and returns nothing — a single wrong keystroke shouldn't empty the dropdown. So when the exact prefix has no good match, the service **expands** it to nearby variants within a small **edit distance** (insertions, deletions, substitutions, transpositions) and looks those up instead, surfacing "netflix" for "netflox" as a *did-you-mean*. Implementations range from generating edit-distance-1/2 candidates and probing the trie, to a dedicated fuzzy structure (a **BK-tree** or an FST with fuzzy matching) or a phonetic index for sound-alikes. It's bounded — edit distance 1–2, only when the exact match is weak — because unbounded fuzziness explodes the candidate set and the latency budget.

The second is **personalization and context**. The global top-k reflects everyone; for a given user, their **own history**, **location**, **language**, and the **current surface** can make different completions more relevant ("home" might mean "Home Depot" near one user and "homework help" for another). The scalable way to do this is *not* a separate trie per user — that doesn't fit in memory — but a **lightweight re-rank**: fetch a slightly larger candidate set from the global trie, then re-order (or splice in a few personal phrases) using per-user signals at query time. This keeps the heavy, shared structure global and small while layering cheap personalization on top. Both refinements trade **precision and cost for recall and relevance**: typo expansion widens what can match (more recall, more compute), personalization tailors the order (more relevance, a little per-user work). The principle: tolerate typos by **bounded edit-distance expansion** only when the exact prefix is weak, and personalize by **re-ranking a global candidate set** with per-user signals rather than maintaining per-user indexes — keeping the shared trie small and fast while still feeling forgiving and tailored.

<h2 id="scalability-evolution">Scalability & Evolution</h2>

Don't build the globally-distributed, sharded, trending-aware system on day one. Each stage answers a specific pressure and adds a specific cost — name the trigger before taking on the complexity.

<TradeoffTable
  columns={["Stage", "Trigger", "Cost"]}
  rows={[
    ["Database LIKE 'prefix%'", "A small catalog, low QPS", "Full scan per keystroke — slow, no ranking, dies under read load"],
    ["In-memory trie on one box", "Thousands of QPS; need ranking", "Precompute top-k per node; one box caps QPS and the index must fit its RAM"],
    ["Replicated trie + batch pipeline + cache", "~1M+ QPS; popularity ranking", "Run a log-aggregation pipeline; replicate the index to many stateless nodes; cache hot prefixes"],
    ["Global, sharded + replicated, real-time trending", "Web scale; freshness + personalization", "Geo-distribution; prefix-sharding when the index outgrows RAM; a real-time layer and edge caching"],
  ]}
  recommendedRow={2}
/>

The progression tracks the wall you're hitting. A **database `LIKE 'prefix%'`** scan is fine for a tiny catalog but does a table scan per keystroke, has no notion of ranking, and collapses under read load — never use it at scale. An **in-memory trie on one box** with precomputed top-k gives you fast, ranked answers, but a single machine caps QPS and the index must fit its RAM. The center of gravity for a real system is a **replicated in-memory trie fed by a batch query-log pipeline, with a suggestion cache** — this is where ranking comes from real popularity, reads scale across stateless replicas, and the hot prefixes are absorbed by the cache. Only true web scale needs **global distribution with prefix-sharding and a real-time trending layer**, paying for geographic reach, an index too big for one node's RAM, and freshness with cross-region complexity. Most autocomplete systems are well-served by "replicated in-memory trie, batch pipeline, cache in front."

<h2 id="resiliency-failure-modes">Resiliency & Failure Modes</h2>

Reason about every dependency in the same matrix — blast radius, detection, mitigation, recovery. Autocomplete's guiding rule is unusual and worth saying out loud: **the dropdown is optional — autocomplete failing must never block the user from typing or submitting a search.** That makes graceful degradation the default posture for almost every failure.

The theme: **degrade, never block; serve a previous good version; filter live.** A dead serving node is harmless because replicas are stateless and each holds a full index copy, so the load balancer just reroutes. A bad build can't poison serving because the index is **immutable and versioned** — you validate before publishing and roll back to the last good version in one step. Overload is met with the cache, client debounce, load-shedding, and the cardinal rule of returning *nothing* rather than being slow. Staleness is covered by the real-time trending layer while the next batch catches up, and ingestion outages are ridden out on the current index. The two safety failures — manipulated trends and unsafe completions — are contained by filtering on **both** paths (build and live query time), so a takedown is immediate. Because the index is a derived, rebuildable artifact and serving is stateless, recovery is almost always "reroute, roll back, or wait for the next build" — and the one inviolable rule is that none of these failures may ever prevent the user from typing and searching.

<h2 id="tradeoffs-alternatives">Trade-offs & Alternatives</h2>

Every recommendation here bought something and gave up something. Stating both is the difference between an answer and a strong answer.

<DecisionRecord
  title="The one-sentence design"
  choice="Serve top-k prefix completions from an in-memory trie whose every node caches its precomputed top-k (so a query is an O(prefix-length) walk plus a list read, never a subtree scan); rank by popularity blended with recency decay, computed offline by a batch pipeline that aggregates the query-log firehose and publishes the index as immutable, atomically-swapped versions; replicate that small (~20 GB) index across stateless serving nodes behind a suggestion cache to absorb ~1M+ QPS within a tight latency budget; patch freshness with a real-time trending layer keyed on velocity; tolerate typos with bounded edit-distance expansion and personalize by re-ranking a global candidate set; and filter unsafe phrases at both build and query time."
  rationale="The hard parts are ranking and read-amplified serving, not matching. Precomputed top-k turns the hot path into constant work; the small index fits in RAM so reads scale by adding stateless replicas with no fan-out; doing all ranking offline keeps the read path trivial and cacheable; immutable versions make serving consistent and rollback instant; the trending layer covers the freshness the batch can't; and two-stage safety filtering reflects that publishing a suggestion is an editorial act. Each choice spends memory or build-time work to buy a fast, safe, scalable read."
  alternatives="A database LIKE 'prefix%' query — trivial but a scan per keystroke, no ranking, dies under read load; computing top-k at query time by scanning the subtree — correct but far too slow for short prefixes at a million QPS; a single non-replicated trie — fast but caps QPS and can't survive a node loss; batch-only freshness — simple but misses trending queries when they matter most; per-user tries for personalization — too large to hold in memory, so re-rank a shared candidate set instead; unbounded fuzzy matching — explodes the candidate set and the latency budget; skipping safety filtering — unacceptable, since autocomplete publishes suggestions to users."
  revisitWhen="The index outgrows a single node's RAM (many languages, entities, personalization) and must be prefix-sharded; freshness requirements tighten enough to need a heavier streaming layer; personalization becomes a primary signal (a learned re-ranker rather than a light re-rank); or the candidate phrases shift from past queries to a structured entity/product catalog with its own ranking."
/>

Four axes capture the real tension:

- **Precompute top-k vs compute at query time.** Precomputing makes reads constant-time at the cost of memory and rebuild-bounded freshness; computing per query is always fresh but far too slow at scale. Precompute wins decisively here.
- **In-memory replicated vs database-backed.** Replicating a small in-memory index gives millisecond reads and trivial horizontal scaling; a database is simpler operationally but can't meet the latency or the QPS. In-memory wins.
- **Batch vs real-time freshness.** Batch gives comprehensive, cheap, decayed popularity but is stale; real-time catches trends instantly but is noisier and gameable. The Lambda split takes both.
- **Global popularity vs personalization.** Global top-k is small, shared, and cache-friendly; personalization is more relevant but costly and doesn't fit a per-user index, so it's layered as a lightweight re-rank on a global candidate set.
