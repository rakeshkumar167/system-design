<h2 id="interview-framing">Interview Framing</h2>

A ticket booking system sells a fixed, finite inventory — the seats at a concert, a flight, a theater — to whoever wants them, and its one inviolable rule is that **the same seat is never sold twice**. A user browses a seat map, picks a seat, pays, and gets a ticket; behind that simple flow is the hardest property in the whole curriculum to guarantee: **correctness under concurrency**. When a popular show opens, far more people than there are seats arrive in the same few seconds, all clicking the same good rows. The system must let exactly one of them win each seat and tell everyone else "taken," instantly, without ever double-selling.

The trap is to treat this as CRUD on an events table. The real problem is that buying takes *time* — you choose a seat, enter payment, the charge takes seconds — yet during that time the seat must be *yours and no one else's*, and if you walk away it must return to the pool. That forces a **reservation** model (hold the seat, then pay), **concurrency control** (so two buyers can't both grab it), and **strong consistency** (so nobody oversells on a stale view). Spend the opening minutes scoping. Good questions to ask:

- Is overselling ever acceptable? (For seats: never. This is a hard invariant, not an SLO.)
- Reserved seating with a seat map, or general admission (a pure count)?
- How long does a user get to hold a seat while paying? What happens if they abandon it?
- How extreme is the contention — a steady trickle, or a million people at a 10am on-sale?
- One person can buy several seats together — is partial success allowed, or all-or-nothing?

<Callout variant="interview" title="Suggested 45-minute allocation">
**5 min** framing and the no-oversell invariant · **5 min** requirements and the seat model · **5 min** capacity (the contention ratio falls out here) · **5 min** entity model and the hold/book API · **10 min** the reservation lifecycle and concurrency control — the centerpiece · **10 min** strong consistency, the payment saga, and hot on-sales · **5 min** failure modes and wrap-up. Say your assumptions out loud — the interviewer is grading whether you can prevent a double-sell under concurrency with an atomic operation and reason about holds and consistency, not whether you can list venues.
</Callout>

This tutorial commits to concrete assumptions so the design stays consistent end to end. **In scope:** the seat/inventory model, the hold → pay → confirm reservation lifecycle, concurrency control (optimistic vs pessimistic), strong consistency on inventory, the idempotent payment saga and compensation, and high-contention on-sale management. **Out of scope:** dynamic/surge pricing, seat-map rendering and UX, fraud-scoring ML, the payment processor's internals (we integrate with one), and resale/transfer marketplaces — name them so the interviewer sees the boundary, then set them aside.

Three targets dominate every later decision. Three related entities complete the picture. An **Event** holds the show's metadata and on-sale time and owns a set of seats. <h2 id="high-level-architecture">High-Level Architecture</h2>

The architecture protects one small, precious thing — the inventory — behind a controlled front door and an authoritative write path, while serving cheap approximate reads from the side.

<TicketBookingArchitecture />

- **Waiting room.** At a hot on-sale, the front door is a queue that admits users into the booking flow at a controlled rate. It converts an un-survivable instantaneous spike into a steady stream the inventory store can actually serve, and it's where fairness (first-come queue position) is enforced.
- **Booking service.** Stateless and replicated. It executes the hold (the atomic conditional update), runs the payment saga, and confirms bookings. It owns no inventory state itself — all coordination is through the inventory store.
- **Inventory store (strongly consistent, sharded by event).** The system of record for seat state, and the only place the no-oversell invariant is enforced. It's strongly consistent so a seat's status is never read stale on the write path, and it's sharded by `event_id` so one hot event's contention is isolated to one shard.
- **Hold expiry worker.** Sweeps for holds past their `held_until` and releases their seats back to `available`, so abandoned holds don't leak inventory. (The hold path also reclaims expired holds lazily, as a backstop.)
- **Payment provider.** External and slow — the reason buying is a saga rather than a single transaction. Charges run against it during confirm.
- **Booking store.** The durable record of completed sales, and the availability cache that serves the high-volume, approximate seat-map reads.

The decisive boundary is **authoritative inventory versus everything else**. The seat status lives in exactly one strongly-consistent place that serializes writes per seat; the waiting room shapes the load hitting it, the cache absorbs reads that don't need to be exact, and the payment saga keeps a slow external charge from holding an inventory transaction open. Every section that follows elaborates how that one authoritative write stays correct under a stampede.

<h2 id="detailed-flows">Detailed Flows</h2>

Four flows cover the system: taking a hold, two buyers contending for the same seat, confirming with payment, and a hold expiring. The contention flow is the one that proves the invariant.

**Hold a seat.** The booking service issues a single atomic conditional update that flips the seat to `held` only if it's still `available`. One round trip decides it: you either reserved the seat or you didn't.

<HoldSeatSequence />

**Contention.** Two users target the same seat at the same instant. Both conditional updates race at the inventory store, but the store serializes them: the first matches the `available` row and wins; the second now finds the row in `held` state, matches **zero** rows, and is rejected with `409`. Exactly one buyer wins — no oversell, no lock held across the network.

<ContentionSequence />

**Confirm with payment.** With a valid hold, the user pays. The booking service charges the external provider, and only on success flips the held seats to `booked` and writes the booking — idempotently, so a retry can't charge twice.

<ConfirmPaymentSequence />

**Hold expiry.** If the user never pays, the hold's TTL passes and the expiry worker releases the seats back to `available`, returning them to the pool for someone else. Without this, abandoned holds would slowly make a sold-out-looking event that isn't.

<h2 id="reservation-lifecycle">Reservation Lifecycle</h2>

A seat moves through a tiny state machine, and getting its transitions right is most of the design. The states are **available → held → booked**, with a path back from `held → available` when a hold expires or is cancelled. `booked` is terminal for the life of the event.

The **hold** exists because buying isn't instantaneous. A user needs time to enter payment, and during that time the seat must be exclusively theirs — otherwise two people would race all the way through checkout and one would be charged for a seat they can't have. The hold reserves the seat *before* payment, decoupling the slow, failure-prone checkout from the inventory decision: the contention is resolved in milliseconds at hold time, and payment then proceeds against a seat that's already guaranteed.

<Callout variant="warning" title="Holds must expire, or inventory leaks">
A hold without a TTL is a memory leak made of seats. Users abandon checkouts constantly — they close the tab, their card fails, they change their mind — and every abandoned hold that never expires is a seat that looks sold but generates no sale and can never be bought. So every hold carries a `held_until`, and seats are reclaimed two ways: an **active sweeper** releases expired holds in the background, and the hold path **lazily** treats a seat whose `held_until` has passed as available (reclaiming it on the spot). Active deletion bounds how long a leak persists; lazy reclaim guarantees an expired hold never actually blocks a sale. It's the same lazy-plus-active pattern the Pastebin tutorial uses for expiry — here it protects revenue.
</Callout>

The hold window is a UX-versus-inventory trade. Too short and users fail to check out in time and lose their seats; too long and abandoned holds tie up inventory during the exact minutes when demand is highest. A few minutes is typical, sometimes shortened dynamically when an event is hot and every held-but-unsold seat is costly.

<h2 id="concurrency-control">Concurrency Control</h2>

This is the centerpiece: how do two buyers *not* both get the same seat? The naive implementation is a **read-modify-write** — read the seat, see it's `available`, then write it to `held` — and it is exactly wrong. Two requests both read `available`, both decide they can take it, and both write `held`: the seat is sold twice. Under a 20:1 contention ratio this race isn't rare, it's the common case. The fix is to never read-then-write; make the reservation a **single atomic operation** that only succeeds if the seat is still free.

**Optimistic concurrency control** is the usual answer. The hold is one conditional update that carries its own precondition:

```sql
UPDATE seats
   SET status = 'held', hold_id = :h, held_until = :t, version = version + 1
 WHERE id = :seat AND status = 'available';
```

The database evaluates the `WHERE` and the write as one atomic, serialized step per row. If the seat was still `available`, **one row** is updated and you won it. If someone beat you, the row is now `held`, the `WHERE` matches **zero rows**, and you know instantly to return `409` — no lock was held, no second read raced. (Equivalently, you can guard on `version`: read the seat with version *v*, then `UPDATE … WHERE id = :seat AND version = :v`; a mismatch means someone changed it under you.) It's called *optimistic* because it assumes conflicts are rare and simply detects the occasional one via the affected-row count, rather than preventing it with a lock up front.

**Pessimistic concurrency control** takes the opposite bet: lock the row first (`SELECT … FOR UPDATE`), then read and write it under the lock, then release. It also prevents the double-sell, and it can be simpler to reason about for multi-row, all-or-nothing holds. But under heavy contention a lock *serializes every contender on a hot seat into a queue*, holds a lock across the transaction, and risks lock waits and deadlocks (especially when one buyer grabs several seats and lock-ordering matters). Optimistic updates have no such held lock — losers fail immediately and cheaply — which is why they're generally preferred for this exact "many buyers, one hot seat" shape.

<h2 id="strong-consistency">Strong Consistency</h2>

The no-oversell invariant is a statement about consistency: every buyer must make their hold decision against the *true, current* state of the seat, not a stale copy. That rules out serving the **inventory of record** from an eventually-consistent store. If the hold path read seat status from an asynchronous replica that lagged the primary by even a second, two buyers could both read `available` from two replicas that hadn't yet seen each other's write — and oversell. So seat state lives in a **strongly consistent (linearizable) store**, and the conditional hold update runs against the authoritative copy, where writes are serialized per seat.

The key move that keeps this affordable is to **separate the authoritative write from the approximate read**. The hold — the thing that must never be wrong — is a single strongly-consistent transaction on one row. The seat-map *display*, by contrast, is read 20× more often and tolerates being a little stale: showing a seat as free a moment after it was taken just means that buyer's hold attempt returns `409`, which the UI already handles. So availability reads come from a cache that's allowed to lag, and only the hold touches the linearizable inventory. You pay for strong consistency exactly where correctness demands it and nowhere else.

<h2 id="payment-saga">Payment & Booking Saga</h2>

Confirming a booking spans two systems that can't share a transaction: your inventory store and an external payment provider that takes seconds and can fail or time out. You can't hold a database transaction open across a slow third-party call, and you can't make the charge and the seat-flip atomic. So booking is a **saga** — a sequence of steps, each with a compensating action — rather than one transaction: the hold reserves the seat, the payment charges the provider, and confirm flips `held → booked`. The hold is what makes this safe: inventory is already reserved before the slow payment runs, so the charge proceeds against a guaranteed seat.

The hard cases are the partial failures, and **idempotency** is what tames them:

- **Payment fails or is declined.** Release the hold (or let it expire) so the seat returns to the pool; the user retries with a different card. No seat is lost, no charge stands.
- **Payment succeeds but confirm fails** (the booking service crashes after the charge). The money moved but the booking didn't — the worst case. The `Idempotency-Key` makes the fix safe: retry the confirm, and because flipping `held → booked` and writing the booking are keyed and idempotent, the retry completes the booking exactly once instead of charging again. A **reconciliation job** backstops it: any payment without a matching booking is either completed or refunded.
- **Client double-submits** the booking. The same idempotency key returns the same booking and the same `200`, never a second charge or a second seat.

<Callout variant="info" title="Idempotency is the glue of the saga">
Every step that can be retried — confirm, the seat flip, even the hold — carries an idempotency key, so 'at least once' execution becomes 'effectively once.' This is the same principle the Notification Service uses for delivery, applied here to money and inventory: because a timed-out call across a system boundary is ambiguous (did the charge land?), you must be able to retry safely, and idempotency is what makes a retry harmless. Without it, every network hiccup risks a double charge or a double booking.
</Callout>

<h2 id="high-contention">High-Contention On-Sales</h2>

The defining operational event is the **on-sale**: a popular show opens and a million people hit "buy" in the same minute. Nothing about steady-state design survives this if you let all that load reach the inventory store at once — the contention collapses into lock waits and timeouts, and legitimate buyers can't get through. Three mechanisms tame it.

- **A virtual waiting room.** Instead of admitting everyone instantly, you queue arrivals and admit them into the booking flow at a controlled rate the inventory can actually serve. The crush becomes a steady stream; users see a fair "you're number 12,000 in line" rather than an error. The waiting room also enforces **fairness** — first-come queue order (or an explicit lottery) — instead of rewarding whoever has the fastest connection.
- **Shard inventory by event.** Each event's seats live on their own shard, so a megastar's on-sale saturates only that event's shard and can't take down bookings for every other event. The shard key is `event_id`, and because a booking never spans events, no cross-shard coordination is needed.
- **Fail fast.** A hold attempt that loses its seat should return `409` immediately and cheaply, freeing the user to try another seat rather than retrying and amplifying load. Fast rejection is a feature: with 19 of 20 attempts doomed to lose, they must lose *quickly*.

The progression maps onto the contention story. When a single hot event can swamp the system, you **shard by event** so its load is isolated and front it with a **waiting room** to shape admission. Only a truly global product needs **multi-region**, and even then each event's inventory keeps a single authoritative home region — because two regions both authoritative over the same seats is, once again, an oversell. Most systems live comfortably at "optimistic + sharded + waiting room."

The theme: **fail closed on inventory, and lean on idempotency for money.** When the inventory store is uncertain, the safe choice is always to *reject* — a rejected sale is recoverable, an oversell is not. And every money-touching step is idempotent and reconciled, so an ambiguous payment call resolves to exactly one charge and one booking rather than zero or two.

<h2 id="security-abuse">Security & Abuse</h2>

A booking system sells scarce, valuable inventory, which makes it a prime target for automated abuse — and the abuse directly harms real buyers by taking their seats.

- **Scalper bots.** Automated buyers try to grab inventory in bulk to resell. Defenses stack: a virtual waiting room with fair ordering, **per-user purchase caps** (e.g. 4 tickets per account per event), CAPTCHAs or proof-of-work at admission, rate limits on holds (the Rate Limiter problem), and device/identity signals to flag bot farms.
- **Hold hoarding.** Even without paying, a bot can deny inventory by holding seats and letting them expire repeatedly, keeping seats out of others' reach. Mitigate with short hold TTLs, limits on concurrent holds per user, and treating rapid hold-and-abandon cycles as abuse.
- **Payment fraud.** Stolen cards and chargebacks. Integrate fraud scoring at the payment step, and because booking is a saga you can decline a confirm without ever having reserved-then-lost the seat dishonestly.
- **Inventory enumeration.** Don't expose internal seat ids or availability counts in a way that lets a scraper map and target the best seats faster than humans; serve the cached, rate-limited seat map.

<Callout variant="info" title="Fairness is a security property here">
Unlike most systems, a booking system's main 'attack' is winning legitimately-shaped requests at inhuman speed. So fairness mechanisms — the waiting room's queue order, per-user caps, rate limits — are not just UX, they're the primary abuse defense. The goal is that a real fan with a normal connection has a fair shot against a bot farm, which is why admission control and purchase caps matter as much as authentication.
</Callout>

<h2 id="tradeoffs-alternatives">Trade-offs & Alternatives</h2>

Every recommendation here bought something and gave up something. Stating both is the difference between an answer and a strong answer.

<DecisionRecord
  title="The one-sentence design"
  choice="Model each seat as a row with a status and version; reserve seats with an atomic optimistic conditional update (rejecting losers with 409); keep inventory in a strongly-consistent store sharded by event; run hold → pay → confirm as an idempotent saga with TTL'd holds released by a sweeper and reclaimed lazily; and gate hot on-sales behind a virtual waiting room with per-user caps."
  rationale="The conditional update makes the no-oversell invariant a single atomic operation with no held lock, so it stays correct and fast even at a 20:1 contention ratio. Strong consistency on inventory is non-negotiable because a stale read is itself an oversell, while caching the seat-map display keeps the dominant read load cheap. The hold decouples slow payment from the inventory decision, and idempotency makes the cross-system saga safe to retry. The waiting room turns an un-survivable spike into a serveable, fair stream."
  alternatives="Pessimistic SELECT … FOR UPDATE locking — simpler reasoning for multi-seat holds, but it serializes contenders and risks deadlocks under load; eventually-consistent inventory — faster reads but it oversells, so it's disqualified; longer hold windows — better checkout UX but more leaked inventory during peak demand; synchronous admission with no waiting room — simpler, but it collapses under a hot on-sale."
  revisitWhen="The contention profile changes materially, the product ever tolerates general-admission counts instead of assigned seats (a simpler atomic decrement), the payment provider's guarantees change, or global expansion forces regional inventory and its consistency trade-offs."
/>

Three axes capture the real tension:

- **Optimistic vs. pessimistic concurrency.** Optimistic conditional updates fail fast with no held lock, ideal for many-buyers-one-seat contention; pessimistic locks are simpler to reason about for multi-row holds but serialize contenders and risk deadlocks. We default optimistic and reach for locks only where multi-seat atomicity is cleaner.
- **Strong vs. eventual consistency.** Inventory must be strongly consistent or it oversells — that's not negotiable. The lever is *scope*: apply strong consistency only to the authoritative hold, and let the high-volume display be eventually-consistent and cached.
- **Hold-window length.** Short holds free abandoned inventory fast but risk timing out real buyers mid-checkout; long holds are friendlier but tie up seats when demand peaks. The right length is event-dependent and sometimes shortened dynamically for hot on-sales.

<h2 id="interview-summary">Interview Summary</h2>

<Callout variant="interview" title="A 60-second end-to-end answer">
"A ticket booking system sells finite inventory under heavy contention, and its one hard rule is never to oversell a seat. I model each seat as a row with a status — available, held, or booked — and a version. The crux is concurrency: a read-then-write would let two buyers both grab the same seat, so instead a hold is a single atomic conditional update, 'UPDATE seat SET status=held WHERE status=available' — exactly one writer matches the row and wins, everyone else updates zero rows and gets a 409, with no lock held across the network. Inventory has to be strongly consistent, because a stale read is itself an oversell; only the seat-map display is cached and approximate. Buying is a saga — hold, then pay an external provider, then confirm held→booked — made safe with idempotency keys so a retry can't double-charge, and holds carry a TTL so abandoned ones are released by a sweeper and reclaimed lazily. For a hot on-sale where a million people chase fifty thousand seats, I put a virtual waiting room in front to admit users at a serveable, fair rate, shard inventory by event to isolate the hot one, and fail losing holds fast. The contention ratio, not raw QPS, is the real challenge."
</Callout>
