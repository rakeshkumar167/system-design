<h2 id="interview-framing">Interview Framing</h2>

A photo-sharing platform — Instagram, Flickr, Google Photos — lets people upload images, processes each into the sizes and formats every device and screen needs, and serves them back in feeds and profiles to a global audience. From the outside it's "upload a picture, show it in a grid," which hides where the real engineering is. Two forces dominate, and neither is the upload button. First, **every upload fans out into many stored artifacts**: you don't serve a phone camera's 4 MB original to a thumbnail grid — you generate a spread of **derivatives** (multiple resolutions, modern formats like WebP/AVIF, cropped variants), so one upload becomes half a dozen stored objects, and at hundreds of millions of uploads a day that's a firehose of new storage. Second, **reads crush writes**: a photo is uploaded once and viewed hundreds of times across feeds, profiles, and re-shares, so the system is overwhelmingly a **read-heavy global delivery** problem, which lives or dies on a **CDN**.

Crucially, this system is built **on top of two you may have already designed**. The bytes live in an [object store](/learn/object-storage) (that tutorial builds the durable blob store this one assumes), and the timeline is a [news feed](/learn/news-feed) fan-out problem (covered in depth there). So the distinctive parts — the parts an interviewer wants *here* — are the **asynchronous image-processing pipeline** and the **CDN-served delivery path**, not re-deriving erasure coding or fan-out. The reframe to lead with: *uploading and feeds are known problems; the design here is a write-amplified, asynchronous image pipeline — one upload becomes an original plus many derivatives, generated off the request path so upload feels instant — feeding a read-dominated delivery path where a CDN serves the overwhelming majority of traffic and the app tier barely touches image bytes. It rests on an object store for durability and a fan-out feed for the timeline; the media pipeline and CDN delivery are the heart of it.*

Spend the opening minutes scoping. Good questions to ask:

- What are we storing and serving — **photos** (immutable image blobs) with feeds and profiles? (Yes — reuse object storage + fan-out feed as substrates.)
- How many **derivatives** per upload, and generated **eagerly** (all on upload) or **on-demand** (lazily, first request)? This shapes the pipeline and storage.
- What's the **read:write ratio** and the **latency** target for viewing? It sizes the CDN and confirms this is a delivery problem.
- **Global** audience? Then a CDN and regional storage matter for latency and cost.
- **Retention** — kept forever? Most photos are viewed heavily for days then rarely, which drives storage tiering.

Three targets shape every later decision. First, **it's a read-heavy delivery problem**: a photo is written once and read hundreds of times, so the design optimizes serving above all — images are **immutable**, so their URLs are **cacheable forever**, and a **CDN** carries the overwhelming majority of reads while the origin object store handles only misses. Second, **processing must be asynchronous**: generating half a dozen derivatives (resize, transcode, strip EXIF) takes real CPU time, and you can't make the user wait, so the upload path just **stores the original and returns**, while a background **pipeline** produces the derivatives — the photo shows a placeholder or the original until they're ready. Third, **storage cost dominates and must be bounded**: each upload amplifies into an original plus many derivatives (the capacity section puts it at ~600 TB/day), most of which is viewed heavily for a few days and then rarely, so the design leans on **tiering** (hot → cold storage), **regenerating** rarely-used derivatives on demand rather than storing them forever, and **deduplication**. Note what's **relaxed**: processing is **eventually** complete (a derivative appearing a second late is fine), and social counters (likes, views) can be **approximate** at scale. The system trades instant-everywhere processing and exact counts for instant uploads, cheap global reads, and bounded storage.

The takeaways. First — the storage lesson — **each upload amplifies**: 100 million uploads a day, each producing an original plus ~5 derivatives (~6 MB total), is ~**600 TB/day** of *new* storage, forever. That's why a durable, cheap **object store** ([Object Storage](/learn/object-storage)) is the foundation and why an **async pipeline** must generate those derivatives — you can't store the flood without an engine producing it, and you can't afford to keep all of it hot forever (hence tiering). Second — the serving lesson — **reads dominate and the CDN absorbs them**: at ~100 views per upload, image serving runs at ~**116,000 requests/sec**, but a **98%-hit CDN** offloads **~50×**, so the origin object store sees only ~**2,300/sec**. The consequence is architectural: **the application tier barely touches image bytes** — it orchestrates uploads and the pipeline and hands clients **CDN URLs**; the heavy lifting of moving pixels to eyeballs happens entirely at the edge. Third, the **upload rate itself is modest** (~1,157/sec) — writes are not the scaling challenge; the *amplification* of each write into storage, and the *volume* of reads, are. The capacity story: size the **storage and pipeline** for write amplification (object store + async derivative generation + tiering) and the **delivery** for read amplification (a CDN doing ~50× offload) — the request-serving app tier is comparatively light.

<h2 id="high-level-architecture">High-Level Architecture</h2>

The architecture is an **async write pipeline** feeding an **object store**, fronted by a **CDN** for reads, with a **metadata DB** and a **feed service** on the side.

<PhotoSharingArchitecture />

- **Client.** Uploads originals (directly to storage) and loads images (directly from the CDN).
- **App / API.** Stateless orchestration: issues presigned upload URLs, writes metadata, enqueues processing jobs, and returns CDN URLs. It moves JSON, not pixels.
- **Object Store.** Durable home of every original and derivative ([Object Storage](/learn/object-storage) is this component). Amplified write volume lands here.
- **Metadata DB.** Photo records, albums, ownership, and social data — small rows pointing at object-store keys; sharded for scale.
- **Image Pipeline.** A queue plus a fleet of workers that read originals and generate derivative sizes/formats asynchronously.
- **CDN.** The delivery tier: caches immutable image URLs at the edge and serves the overwhelming majority of reads, filling from the object store on a miss.

The decisive boundary is that **writes flow into an object store through an async pipeline while reads flow out of a CDN — the app tier orchestrates but never carries image bytes**. Every section that follows elaborates one piece: the direct-to-storage upload, the image pipeline, CDN/responsive serving, how the feed plugs in, metadata/social, and storage tiering.

<h2 id="upload-path">Upload: Direct-to-Storage & Presigned URLs</h2>

The first non-obvious decision is how the bytes get in. The naive design streams the upload **through** the application servers: client → API → object store. At photo scale that's a mistake — a 4 MB body times thousands of uploads a second means your app tier is spending all its CPU and network **proxying pixels**, a job it adds no value to, and you'd have to scale the app fleet for byte-pushing rather than logic. The fix is **direct-to-storage upload via a presigned URL**: the client asks the API to *initiate* an upload, the API creates the photo record and returns a **short-lived, signed URL** that grants permission to `PUT` the original straight to the object store, and the client uploads the bytes **directly**, bypassing the app entirely. The API only ever handles small JSON; the object store handles the big bytes it's built for.

The second decision is that **processing is asynchronous**, which is what makes the upload feel instant. When the upload to storage completes (signaled by a storage event or a small confirm call), the photo transitions to `processing` and a job is enqueued for the pipeline — and the user's upload is **already done**, showing the original or a placeholder while derivatives generate in the background. This decoupling is the same async-queue pattern as the [Notification Service](/learn/notification-service) and [News Feed](/learn/news-feed) fan-out: accept fast, do the slow work behind a queue. (Large originals can additionally use **multipart upload** from the [Object Storage](/learn/object-storage) tutorial.) The principle: **upload originals directly to the object store via presigned URLs** so the app tier never proxies image bytes, and **process asynchronously** so the upload returns instantly while derivative generation happens off the request path.

<h2 id="image-pipeline">The Image Processing Pipeline</h2>

This is the centerpiece. Once an original is stored, an **asynchronous, event-driven pipeline** turns it into everything the product actually serves. A queue holds processing jobs, and a fleet of **workers** consumes them; each worker reads the original and **generates the derivatives**.

<ProcessImageSequence />

What a worker produces per photo is a small matrix: multiple **resolutions** (a tiny thumbnail for grids, small/medium for feeds, large for detail views — you never serve a 4 MB original to a 150-pixel thumbnail), modern **formats** (WebP and AVIF, which are far smaller than JPEG for the same quality, with a JPEG fallback for old clients), maybe **crops** (a square for the grid), and often a **blurhash / low-quality placeholder** for instant perceived loading. It also **strips EXIF metadata** from the image — both for privacy (originals embed GPS location, device info) and to shrink the file. Two properties make the pipeline robust. It's **idempotent**: a job that's retried after a worker crash (at-least-once delivery from the queue) re-derives exactly the same outputs to the same keys, so duplicates are harmless — the same discipline as the [Distributed Job Scheduler](/learn/distributed-job-scheduler). And the **original is never modified**: derivatives are pure functions of it, so they're **regenerable** — if you later add a new size or a new format (AVIF didn't exist when you built the product), you backfill by re-running the pipeline over originals, and if you drop a rarely-used derivative to save storage you can recreate it on demand. The trade-off is **eager vs lazy** generation: eagerly producing all derivatives on upload makes every read a cache hit but stores renditions that may never be viewed; lazily generating on first request saves storage but adds latency to the first view (often the best answer is eager for common sizes, lazy for rare ones). The principle: an **asynchronous, idempotent worker pipeline** generates the derivative resolutions and formats (and strips EXIF) from an **immutable original**, so derivatives are **regenerable cached artifacts** — decoupling slow processing from the upload and letting formats and sizes evolve without touching originals.

<h2 id="serving-cdn">Serving Images: CDN & Responsive Delivery</h2>

Serving is where the read amplification is tamed, and the answer is a **CDN**. Because images are **immutable** — a derivative at a given URL never changes — they are perfectly cacheable, so a CDN can hold them at edge locations **close to users worldwide** and serve them without ever touching the origin. As the capacity model showed, a ~98% edge hit rate means the object store sees only ~2% of reads (a **~50× offload**), which is what makes globally serving hundreds of thousands of image requests per second **affordable and fast** — the bytes travel from a nearby edge, not a distant origin. The origin (the object store) is consulted only on a **cache miss** (a cold or newly-generated image), after which the edge caches it for everyone nearby. This is the same delivery tier as the [Video Streaming](/learn/video-streaming) platform, applied to images.

The second lever is **responsive delivery**: serve the **smallest derivative that fits the context**. A phone showing a grid of thumbnails should get 150-pixel WebP thumbnails, not full-resolution originals — sending the right size saves enormous bandwidth (and time and battery) across billions of views. The client picks the rendition from the per-size URLs the API returned, or an image CDN selects it from the request (device, viewport, `Accept` header for format negotiation — WebP/AVIF to capable browsers, JPEG to the rest). Combined, **immutable + right-sized + edge-cached** is why a feed of images loads instantly worldwide. The trade-off is **cache invalidation** — but immutability sidesteps it: since a URL's content never changes, you never invalidate, you just publish a **new URL** for new content (a deleted photo is handled by removing access, not by mutating a cached object). The principle: serve images from a **CDN** that caches immutable per-size URLs at the edge (absorbing ~98% of reads), and use **responsive delivery** to send the smallest derivative and best format for each device — turning read-dominated global image serving into cheap, fast edge delivery with no cache-invalidation problem.

<h2 id="feed-generation">Feed Generation</h2>

The timeline — the scroll of photos from people you follow — is a **feed fan-out** problem, and it's exactly the [News Feed](/learn/news-feed) tutorial, so the right move in an interview is to **name it and reuse it** rather than re-derive it. The short version: you choose between **fan-out on write** (push each new photo's ID into every follower's precomputed feed, so reads are cheap O(1) lookups at the cost of write amplification), **fan-out on read** (assemble the feed at read time by pulling from followed users, cheap writes but expensive reads), and a **hybrid** that pushes for normal users but **pulls for celebrities** with millions of followers (whose push would be catastrophic) — all covered in depth there.

<FeedLoadSequence />

The **photo-specific** point worth making here is what the feed stores and returns: **post IDs, not image bytes**. A feed entry is a lightweight reference — a `photo_id` plus a bit of metadata — that is **hydrated** at read time into the metadata (caption, owner, counts) and, crucially, **CDN image URLs**. The feed service therefore serves only small JSON; the actual image pixels load **separately, directly from the CDN**. This separation is what keeps feeds fast and the feed service cheap: it never touches an image byte, and the heavy media delivery is handled by the edge exactly as in the serving section. The principle: **feed generation is the fan-out problem from the News Feed design** (push/pull/hybrid), and here the feed stores **post IDs hydrated with CDN image URLs** — so the feed service moves lightweight references while the images themselves are served from the edge.

<h2 id="metadata-social">Metadata, Albums & Social Interactions</h2>

Around the images sits a conventional but high-scale metadata and social layer. The **metadata DB** holds the small photo records (ownership, caption, derivative keys, status), **albums** (collections referencing `photo_id`s), and the social graph (follows) — all small rows, but *many* of them, so this is a **sharded** datastore in its own right (by user or photo id), which is the [Distributed Key-Value Store](/learn/object-storage) scaling shape. It's on the read path (a feed hydrates from it), so it's cached and read-optimized.

The interesting scaling wrinkle is **social interactions** — likes, comments, views. A viral photo can get **millions of likes**, and computing "how many likes does this photo have?" with a live `COUNT(*)` over a likes table on every feed render would be ruinous. So counts are **denormalized into per-photo counters** and updated as interactions arrive (incrementing a counter, the [Leaderboard](/learn/leaderboard)'s sorted-set/counter pattern), and at extreme scale they're **approximate** — displaying "2.4M likes" doesn't require exactness, so counters can be aggregated, sampled, or eventually consistent. Comments are stored and paginated like any high-write append log. The write volume of interactions often **dwarfs** the photo uploads themselves (far more likes than posts), making this its own firehose. The principle: the **metadata and social layer is a sharded, read-optimized datastore** of many small records, with **social interaction counts denormalized into (often approximate) per-photo counters** rather than computed live — because at viral scale, exact real-time counts are neither affordable nor necessary.

<h2 id="storage-tiering">Storage, Tiering & Cost</h2>

At ~600 TB of new data every day that lives essentially forever, **storage cost becomes the dominant operational expense**, and the design has to actively manage it. The key observation is that photo access is **extremely skewed by age**: a photo is viewed heavily in the days after it's posted (feeds, notifications) and then almost never — last year's vacation photos sit untouched for years but must still be retained. That skew is a gift, because it means most of your stored bytes are **cold** and don't need to sit on fast, expensive storage.

Three levers manage the cost. First, **tiering**: move objects from **hot** storage (fast, expensive) to progressively **colder** tiers (cheaper, higher-latency — the object store's storage classes, "standard → infrequent-access → archive") based on age or access, via **lifecycle policies** — a rarely-touched photo costs a fraction to keep in cold storage, and the slightly higher retrieval latency is fine for an old photo. Second, **regenerate rather than store rarely-used derivatives**: since derivatives are reproducible from the original, you can keep only the common sizes hot and **recreate uncommon ones on demand** (lazy generation), trading a little compute for a lot of storage — you don't need to store a "large" rendition of a five-year-old photo nobody views. Third, **deduplication**: identical uploads (the same meme re-shared thousands of times) can be detected by **content hash** and stored **once**, with many photo records pointing at the same blob. The **originals**, though, are always kept **durably** (erasure-coded in the object store) — they're the source of truth from which everything else regenerates. The trade-off is **cost vs retrieval latency/compute**: cold tiers and on-demand regeneration are cheaper but slower on the rare access, which is exactly the right bargain for cold data. The principle: exploit the **age-skewed access pattern** to bound cost — **tier** hot→cold with lifecycle policies, **regenerate** rarely-used derivatives instead of storing them, and **deduplicate** identical content — while always keeping **originals durably** as the regenerable source of truth.

<h2 id="scalability-evolution">Scalability & Evolution</h2>

Don't build the globally-distributed, tiered, multi-format pipeline on day one. Each stage answers a specific pressure and adds a specific cost — name the trigger before taking on the complexity.

<TradeoffTable
  columns={["Stage", "Trigger", "Cost"]}
  rows={[
    ["App server + local disk", "A few users, small volume", "Simple, but no durability, no scale, and it proxies every byte — a toy"],
    ["Object store + CDN, synchronous resize", "Real traffic; global reads", "Durable storage and edge delivery, but resizing on the upload path makes uploads slow"],
    ["Async image pipeline + sharded metadata", "High upload volume; instant-upload UX", "A queue + worker fleet; metadata sharding; eventual derivative readiness"],
    ["Global multi-region + storage tiering", "Worldwide audience; cost pressure", "Regional storage + CDN; hot/cold lifecycle tiering; dedup; on-demand regeneration"],
  ]}
  recommendedRow={2}
/>

The progression tracks the wall you're hitting. An **app server with local disk** is a toy — no durability, and it proxies every byte. Putting an **object store behind a CDN** is the first real step (durable bytes, edge delivery), but resizing **synchronously on upload** makes uploads slow and couples the app tier to heavy CPU work. The center of gravity is an **async image pipeline with sharded metadata** — direct-to-storage uploads, background derivative generation, and a metadata store that scales — which is where instant uploads and read-scale serving come from and where most of this design sits. Only a worldwide product at cost pressure needs **multi-region storage and tiering** — regional object stores and CDNs for locality, and hot/cold lifecycle policies plus dedup and on-demand regeneration to bound the ever-growing storage bill. Most photo platforms are well-served by "object store + CDN + async pipeline + sharded metadata," adding regions and tiers as reach and cost demand.

<h2 id="resiliency-failure-modes">Resiliency & Failure Modes</h2>

Reason about every dependency in the same matrix — blast radius, detection, mitigation, recovery. The photo platform's guiding rule: **never lose an original (everything else is regenerable), and degrade the experience rather than break it** — a missing derivative or a processing delay should show the original or a placeholder, not an error.

  rows={[
    { failure: "Pipeline worker crash mid-job", impact: "A photo's derivatives delayed", detection: "Job visibility timeout / queue redelivery", mitigation: "At-least-once queue redelivers; jobs are idempotent", recovery: "Another worker re-runs the job; derivatives appear" },

The theme: **originals are sacred and regenerable-everything, reads degrade gracefully, processing is retryable.** A crashed worker is harmless because the queue is **at-least-once** and jobs are **idempotent** — another worker re-runs it. A processing backlog degrades gently: show the **original or a placeholder** while derivatives catch up, since readiness is eventual by design. Object-store trouble is bounded because it's the **durable, erasure-coded substrate** ([Object Storage](/learn/object-storage)) and the CDN keeps serving cached reads; a CDN outage is met with **multi-CDN failover** and a rate-limited origin. A missing derivative isn't an error — you **fall back** to the original or another size and **lazily regenerate** the requested one (regenerability pays off again). Metadata loss is handled like any replicated store. And a viral photo is *easy* here precisely because images are immutable and **CDN-cacheable**, so a read storm is absorbed at the edge. Because originals are durable and everything else is reproducible, recovery is almost always "retry the job, fail over, or regenerate" — and the one line never crossed is losing an original.

<h2 id="tradeoffs-alternatives">Trade-offs & Alternatives</h2>

Every recommendation here bought something and gave up something. Stating both is the difference between an answer and a strong answer.

<DecisionRecord
  title="The one-sentence design"
  choice="Upload originals directly to an object store via presigned URLs (never proxying bytes through the app), record small metadata rows pointing at object-store keys, and generate derivative sizes/formats with an asynchronous, idempotent worker pipeline so uploads feel instant and derivatives are regenerable from the immutable original; serve images from a CDN of immutable, per-size URLs (absorbing ~98% of reads) with responsive delivery of the smallest fitting rendition; build the timeline as a fan-out feed (per the News Feed design) that stores post IDs hydrated with CDN image URLs; keep social counts as denormalized, approximate per-photo counters; and bound storage cost with hot→cold tiering, dedup by content hash, and on-demand regeneration of rarely-used derivatives — always keeping originals durably in the object store."
  rationale="The system is write-amplified in storage and read-amplified in serving, so the design offloads byte movement to the object store (write) and the CDN (read) and keeps the app tier light. Async, idempotent processing decouples slow derivative generation from the upload and makes derivatives regenerable, so formats/sizes can evolve and cost can be managed by tiering and regeneration. Immutability makes images perfectly cacheable (no invalidation) and gives cheap, fast global delivery. Reusing object storage and fan-out feeds avoids rebuilding solved problems, focusing effort on the media pipeline and delivery that are distinctive."
  alternatives="Proxy uploads through the app tier — simple but burns app CPU/network on byte-pushing and scales poorly. Resize synchronously on upload — no pipeline, but slow uploads and coupled heavy CPU. Store every derivative forever — every read is a hit but storage cost balloons; regeneration/tiering is cheaper. No CDN — impossible at read scale; the origin can't serve global image traffic affordably. Rebuild fan-out or object storage here — wasteful; reuse the News Feed and Object Storage designs. Exact live like counts — ruinous at viral scale; approximate counters suffice. Mutable image URLs — reintroduce cache invalidation that immutability avoids."
  revisitWhen="Video is added (a distinct transcoding pipeline and player). ML features arrive (auto-tagging, ranking, dedup by perceptual hash). The audience goes global (multi-region storage + CDN, regional compliance). Storage cost outpaces revenue (more aggressive tiering, drop-and-regenerate more derivatives, better dedup). Or upload patterns shift to huge originals (lean harder on multipart and lazy derivative generation)."
/>

Four axes capture the real tension:

- **Eager vs lazy derivative generation.** Eager (all sizes on upload) makes every read a hit but stores renditions that may never be viewed; lazy (on first request) saves storage but adds first-view latency. Often eager for common sizes, lazy for rare ones.
- **Direct-to-storage vs proxied upload.** Direct (presigned URL) keeps the app tier light and uploads fast; proxied is simpler to reason about but wastes app resources on byte-pushing. Direct wins at scale.
- **Store all derivatives vs regenerate.** Storing everything is fast to serve but expensive; regenerating rarely-used sizes trades compute for storage. Regenerate the cold tail.
- **Exact vs approximate social counts.** Exact is truthful but ruinous at viral scale; approximate counters are cheap and indistinguishable to users. Approximate wins for display counts.

<Callout variant="interview" title="A 60-second end-to-end answer">
"A photo-sharing platform looks like upload-and-show, but the real design is a write-amplified async image pipeline feeding a read-dominated CDN delivery path, and it rests on two systems I'd reuse rather than rebuild: an object store for the bytes and a fan-out feed for the timeline.
