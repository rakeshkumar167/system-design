<h2 id="interview-framing">Interview Framing</h2>

A news feed is the home timeline you see when you open Facebook, Twitter/X, Instagram, or LinkedIn: a personalized, ranked list of recent posts from the accounts you follow. You follow some people; they post; their posts show up in your feed, newest and most relevant first; you scroll, and more load. From the outside it's a textbook database query — "select posts where author in (the people I follow) order by created_at desc limit 50" — and that framing produces something that works for a hundred users and a thousand posts and then becomes impossible at a billion users: each feed open would scatter a query across hundreds of followees living on many shards, merge and sort the results on the fly, and do this for billions of feed opens a day. The query is simple; running it at scale, on every refresh, is the wall.

The hard part is that **read-time aggregation is too expensive at scale, so you precompute each user's feed — and that precomputation has its own problems**. Three things dominate, and the database query is the easy one. First, **fan-out**: rather than aggregate at read time (**pull**), you aggregate at *write* time (**push**) — when someone posts, you push that post into the precomputed feed of every one of their followers, so a feed read becomes a single O(1) lookup of a ready-made list. That trades a cheap, rare write for an expensive, frequent read — the right trade, because feeds are read far more than written and must feel instant — but it creates enormous **write amplification** (one post becomes as many feed writes as the author has followers). Second, **hot users**: pure push **breaks for celebrities** — a post by an account with 100 million followers is 100 million feed writes, most of them landing in feeds nobody will read that day — so real systems go **hybrid**: push for ordinary accounts, but for high-fan-out accounts *don't* push; instead **pull** their recent posts at read time and **merge** them in. Third, **ranking and storage**: modern feeds aren't chronological — they're **relevance-ranked** by affinity, recency, and predicted engagement — and the precomputed feed stores only **post IDs**, not content, which are **hydrated** from a post store at read time so an edit or delete is reflected once instead of being copied into millions of feeds. The reframe to lead with: *it looks like a query over the people you follow, but that query is impossible to run at scale on every read — so you precompute feeds by fanning out on write, go hybrid for celebrities to bound the write amplification, rank for relevance, and store feeds as lists of post IDs hydrated from a post store. Reading a feed is trivial once it's precomputed; precomputing it cheaply, for everyone, including the accounts with a hundred million followers, is the design.*

Spend the opening minutes scoping. Good questions to ask:

- Chronological or ranked? Ranking changes the read path (a scoring step) and is what most modern feeds do.
- How big — average followers, and how skewed? The skew (celebrities) is what forces the hybrid model.
- How fresh must posts be — seconds, or is a short delay fine? Fan-out is asynchronous, so feeds are eventually consistent.
- Read-heavy or write-heavy from the user's view? (Reads dominate user behavior, but push makes the *system* write-heavy — worth naming.)
- One feed type, or many surfaces (home, profile, explore)? We'll design the home feed.

Three targets shape every later decision. First, **the feed read must be instant**: people open and refresh their feed constantly, so the read path has to be a fast lookup, not a multi-shard aggregation — which is the whole reason to **precompute** feeds. Second, **fan-out makes the system write-heavy even though the product is read-heavy**: pushing each post to all followers means a moderate post rate becomes a huge feed-write rate (the capacity section puts it at ~300×), so the fan-out must be an **asynchronous, horizontally-scaled pipeline** that posting latency never waits on. Third, **freshness is eventual, and availability beats consistency**: because fan-out is async, a brand-new post may take a few seconds to appear in every follower's feed — perfectly acceptable for a timeline — and if something fails, the feed must still **render** (serve a slightly stale or cached version) rather than error, because a blank feed is a far worse experience than an outdated one. Note what's *relaxed*: there's **no strict ordering or exactly-once** requirement (an occasional duplicate filtered by post ID, or a post arriving a second late, is fine), and **coverage can be lazy** (feeds for inactive users needn't be eagerly maintained). The system trades strict freshness and consistency for instant reads, bounded write cost, and an always-rendering feed.

And the amplification is exactly why **celebrities break pure push**: an account with 100 million followers turns *one* post into 100 million feed writes, a spike no push pipeline should absorb — which forces the **hybrid** model. The capacity story: the feed is hard because of **fan-out write amplification and ranking**, not because reading a precomputed list is difficult.

Two supporting entities complete the picture. <h2 id="high-level-architecture">High-Level Architecture</h2>

The architecture is two planes that meet at the per-user feed: a **write/fan-out plane** that pushes new posts into followers' precomputed feeds, and a **read plane** that serves those feeds and hydrates them into full posts.

<NewsFeedArchitecture />

- **Client.** Posts content and reads the feed; paginates by cursor.
- **Post Service.** Persists a post to the post store and triggers fan-out. Returns fast.
- **Post Store.** The durable home of every post (content, media reference, author, counters) — the source of truth, read once per hydration.
- **Fan-out Service.** On a new post, reads the author's followers from the social graph and enqueues fan-out jobs. Decides push vs hybrid based on the author's follower count.
- **Social Graph.** The follow edges — who follows whom — a dependency consulted by fan-out and the hybrid read.
- **Fan-out Queue.** Decouples posting from fan-out so spikes are absorbed and posting never waits.
- **Fan-out Workers.** Consume jobs and write the post ID into each follower's feed (batched, sharded by user). Idempotent — a retry can't duplicate an entry.
- **Feed Cache.** The precomputed per-user feeds — bounded lists of post IDs + scores — in a fast in-memory store. A read is one lookup here.
- **Feed Service.** Reads the precomputed feed, merges pulled hot-user posts, hydrates content from the post store, ranks, and returns a page.

The decisive boundary is **the write plane precomputes feeds by fanning out on write, and the read plane serves and hydrates them** — connected by the per-user feed cache, which is derived state over the durable post store and graph. Fan-out is asynchronous (queue + workers) so posting stays fast and spikes are absorbed; feeds store IDs so they're tiny and a post edit is one write; the read path hydrates and ranks over a small candidate set; and the hybrid decision (push vs pull) lives in the fan-out and read services so celebrities don't trigger a write storm. Every section that follows elaborates one piece: the push/pull choice, the hybrid for hot users, ranking, storage and hydration, the fan-out pipeline, and the read path.

<h2 id="fanout-models">Fan-out: Write vs Read</h2>

This is the centerpiece, and the whole design pivots on it. There are two ways to assemble a feed. **Fan-out on read (pull)**: store each post once, and when a user opens their feed, query all the accounts they follow, merge the results, and sort — assembling the feed *at read time*. It's simple and storage-cheap (one copy of each post, no precomputed feeds), and it's always fresh, but every single read does an expensive **scatter-gather** across hundreds of followees on many shards, and feeds are read constantly — so this melts under read load. **Fan-out on write (push)**: when a post is created, immediately write its ID into the precomputed feed of every follower, so when a user opens their feed it's a **single O(1) lookup** of a ready-made list. Reads become trivial; the cost moves to writes, which now carry the fan-out **amplification** (one post → as many feed writes as the author has followers) and do some wasted work (pushing into feeds of users who won't read them soon).

The choice comes down to **where you can afford to pay**: feeds are read far more often than they're written, and reads are the latency-critical, user-facing operation, so you want reads cheap — which means **push is the default for the body of users**. You spend extra, latency-tolerant work at write time (precomputing feeds) to make the frequent, latency-critical read a simple lookup; it's the same precompute-for-fast-reads trade as **Search Autocomplete**'s top-k. Pull isn't useless, though — it's exactly right when fan-out would be wasteful: rarely-read feeds (inactive users) and, crucially, **very high fan-out accounts** (the next section), where pushing to tens of millions of followers costs far more than pulling on demand. The principle: **fan-out on read is simple but pays on every read; fan-out on write precomputes feeds so reads are O(1) but pays write amplification** — and because reads dominate and must be instant, push is the default, with pull reserved for where push is wasteful.

<h2 id="hybrid-hot-users">The Hybrid Model & Hot Users</h2>

Pure push has a fatal edge case: **celebrities**. An account with 100 million followers turns a *single* post into 100 million feed writes — an enormous, spiky burst of work, most of it landing in the feeds of users who won't open the app before the post is stale. The amplification that's merely large for an average account (hundreds of followers) becomes catastrophic at the top of the follower distribution, which is heavily **skewed**: a tiny number of accounts have a vast share of the follows. Pushing for them wastes compute and storage and creates thundering-herd write spikes whenever they post.

The fix is the **hybrid model**: **push for ordinary accounts, pull for hot ones**. Most of everyone's feed is still precomputed by fan-out-on-write, as before. But for a small set of **high-fan-out accounts** — identified by a follower-count threshold — the system **does not push** their posts; instead, at **read time**, the feed service **pulls** those accounts' recent posts directly and **merges** them into the reader's precomputed feed before ranking. Because a user follows only a handful of celebrities, this read-time pull is cheap (a few extra lookups), and it eliminates the millions of wasteful writes. The threshold is a tuning knob: set it where push amplification stops being worth it. Related optimizations ride along — feeds for **inactive users** can be built lazily (pulled on their rare visits rather than eagerly pushed), and a newly-**followed** account's back-catalog can be pulled rather than back-pushed. The principle: bound write amplification by **not pushing high-fan-out accounts** — push the common case for cheap reads, **pull-and-merge the celebrity tail at read time**, and tune the threshold so neither the write storm of full push nor the read cost of full pull dominates.

<h2 id="feed-ranking">Feed Ranking</h2>

Early feeds were **chronological** — newest first — which is simple, predictable, and easy to reason about, but it buries a post you'd love under a pile of recent noise and shows you a stale-but-recent item over a highly relevant older one. Modern feeds are **relevance-ranked**: after the candidate posts are gathered (the pushed feed plus any pulled hot-user posts), each is **scored**, and the feed is ordered by score rather than time. The signals are familiar "EdgeRank"-style features: **affinity** (how close the reader is to the author — past interactions, follows-back, messaging), **recency** (a time-decay so fresh content is favored without being absolute), **predicted engagement** (the model's estimate that you'll like, comment, share, or dwell), and **content-type weighting** (photos vs links vs text). The score combines these, and the top-scoring posts lead the feed.

Ranking changes the read path: instead of returning a precomputed list verbatim, the feed service **scores a bounded candidate set** at read time (often a few hundred candidates) and returns the best page. To keep that affordable, the candidate set is kept small (the bounded precomputed feed plus a few pulled accounts), some features are **precomputed** (affinity tables, post quality), and the heavy model work is bounded per request. The trade-off is **engagement and relevance versus simplicity and predictability**: ranked feeds demonstrably increase engagement but add a scoring stage, model infrastructure, and the well-known downsides (filter bubbles, harder debugging, "why am I seeing this?"). The principle: rank a **bounded candidate set** by **affinity + recency + predicted engagement** at read time, precomputing features to stay within the latency budget — choosing relevance ranking over chronological because it drives engagement, while acknowledging the added complexity and the option to fall back to chronological when ranking is unavailable.

<h2 id="feed-storage-hydration">Feed Storage, Hydration & Caching</h2>

A crucial design decision is *what* the precomputed feed actually stores. It stores **post IDs (plus a score), not post content**. Each user's feed is a **bounded list** — the most recent ~1,000 entries — held in a fast in-memory store (e.g. Redis sorted sets keyed by user), which is why all of 300M users' feeds fit in only ~9.6 TB: an entry is a reference, not a copy. The post **content** — text, media, author, like/comment counts — lives **once** in the **post store**, and at read time the feed's IDs are **hydrated** into full posts by looking them up there (through a post cache). This separation is the payoff of storing IDs: when a post is **edited, deleted, or its counters change**, you update **one** record in the post store and every feed that references it reflects the change on the next hydration — instead of having copied the post into millions of feeds and needing to rewrite them all.

Caching is layered to protect both the read and the write paths. **Active-user feeds** stay hot in the feed cache; **popular posts** are cached in the post store's read cache so hydration is cheap even when a celebrity's post is referenced by millions of feeds (it's read from cache, not recomputed). The bounded feed length caps memory and matches reality — nobody scrolls past the recent window, and older content can be fetched on demand. Because the feed cache is **derived state** over the durable post store and graph, losing a feed shard is recoverable: the affected feeds are **rebuilt** from recent posts and the follow graph. The principle: store feeds as **bounded lists of IDs + scores**, keep post **content in one place** and **hydrate** at read time (so edits/deletes touch one record), and **cache** active feeds and hot posts — treating the feed store as a rebuildable cache, not a system of record.

<h2 id="fanout-pipeline">The Fan-out Pipeline</h2>

Posting must feel instant, so fan-out — the expensive part — is done **asynchronously**. When a post is created, the post service does two cheap things: **persist** the post to the post store, and **enqueue** a fan-out job; then it returns. Behind the queue, a fleet of **fan-out workers** consumes jobs, looks up the author's followers from the **social graph**, and writes the post ID into each follower's feed — **batched** (many followers per round-trip) and **sharded by user** (each worker owns a slice of feeds), which is how ~174 workers sustain ~1.74M feed writes/sec. This decoupling means posting latency never depends on follower count, and the queue **absorbs spikes** (a burst of posts queues up and drains rather than overwhelming the feed store) — the same async **queue/worker fan-out pattern** as the **Notification Service**.

Reliability follows the queue's usual discipline. Workers process jobs **at-least-once**, so a crash mid-fan-out just re-leases the job to another worker; the feed writes are **idempotent** — keyed by `(user_id, post_id)` — so a retry that re-pushes an already-delivered post is a no-op rather than a duplicate. Fan-out can be **prioritized**: push to **active users first** so the people likely to open the app soon see the post quickly, and lazily handle inactive users (or skip them, pulling on their rare visit). And the hybrid decision lives here — workers simply **don't fan out** high-follower accounts, leaving those to read-time pull. The principle: make fan-out an **async, queue-backed, horizontally-sharded pipeline** so posting is fast and spikes are absorbed, with **at-least-once delivery and idempotent feed writes** for safe retries, **active-user prioritization** for perceived freshness, and the **hybrid skip** for hot accounts — the Notification Service's fan-out pattern applied to timelines.

<h2 id="read-path-consistency">Read Path: Pagination & Freshness</h2>

Reading a feed pulls the pieces together. The feed service **reads the precomputed feed** (a list of post IDs) in one lookup, **merges** any pulled hot-user posts (the hybrid step), **hydrates** the IDs into full posts from the post store, **ranks** the bounded candidate set, and returns a **page**. Pagination uses an **opaque cursor** rather than a numeric offset, so the page is stable as new posts arrive at the top — the cursor encodes a position (a score/timestamp boundary) and the next page continues from there, avoiding the "items shift and you see duplicates or gaps" problem that offset pagination has on a constantly-changing list. The read path also handles **dedup** (the same post reachable via push and pull, or across pages, is shown once — easy because entries are keyed by post ID) and optional **already-seen** filtering (don't re-surface posts the user has already scrolled past).

The freshness model is **eventual consistency**, and that's a deliberate, acceptable relaxation. Because fan-out is asynchronous, a just-created post takes a **few seconds** to appear in every follower's feed — fine for a timeline, where no one expects instantaneous global propagation. Two touches improve the *perceived* freshness: the reader's **own** new post can be **injected immediately** into their view (so posting feels instant to the author even before fan-out completes), and feeds can be **refreshed** on pull-to-refresh to pick up the latest pushed entries. Crucially, the read path is built to **always render**: if ranking is down it falls back to chronological, if a hydration misses it skips that item, and if the live feed is unavailable it serves the last cached version — because a slightly stale feed is vastly better than a blank one. The principle: assemble the page by **reading + merging + hydrating + ranking**, paginate with a **stable cursor**, **dedup by post ID**, and accept **eventual freshness** while protecting *perceived* freshness (inject the user's own post) and *availability* (always render — degrade to cached/chronological).

<h2 id="scalability-evolution">Scalability & Evolution</h2>

Don't build the globally-sharded, hybrid, ranked feed on day one. Each stage answers a specific pressure and adds a specific cost — name the trigger before taking on the complexity.

The progression tracks the wall you're hitting. **Pull** (read-time aggregation) is the obvious first build and fine for a small graph, but the scatter-gather cost grows with both users and follows until reads collapse. **Push** (fan-out-on-write) makes reads instant by precomputing feeds, at the price of write amplification and the fan-out pipeline — the right move once reads dominate. The center of gravity for a real feed is **hybrid fan-out with ranking and caching**: push for the body of users, pull-and-merge celebrities, rank for relevance, and cache active feeds and hot posts — this is where the write storm is bounded and the feed feels both fast and relevant. Only true global scale needs **geo-sharded feeds with regional caches**, paying for cross-region freshness and the sheer capacity of the fan-out firehose. Most feeds are well-served by "hybrid fan-out, relevance ranking, IDs-hydrated-from-a-post-store, heavy caching."

The feed's guiding rule is unusual and worth saying out loud: **the feed must always render — degrade to cached, stale, or chronological rather than fail**, because a blank feed is the worst possible outcome.

The theme: **degrade gracefully, lean on derived state, and bound the tail.** A dead fan-out worker is harmless because jobs are **at-least-once** and feed writes are **idempotent**, so re-leasing finishes the work without duplicates. Backlogs are met by **prioritizing active users** and scaling workers while the current (slightly stale) feed keeps serving. A lost feed shard is recoverable precisely because feeds are **derived state** — rebuildable from the durable post store and graph (or pulled on demand). Post-store hiccups are ridden out by the **post cache**, skipping any item that can't be hydrated rather than failing the page. The celebrity write storm is **designed out** by the hybrid model, and a downed ranking service simply **falls back to chronological**. Because the feed cache is rebuildable and every read path has a degraded mode, recovery is almost always "re-lease, rebuild, or fall back" — and the one inviolable rule is that the feed always renders *something*, even if stale or chronological.

<h2 id="tradeoffs-alternatives">Trade-offs & Alternatives</h2>

Every recommendation here bought something and gave up something. Stating both is the difference between an answer and a strong answer.

<DecisionRecord
  title="The one-sentence design"
  choice="Precompute each user's feed by fanning out posts on write into bounded per-user lists of post IDs + scores held in a fast cache (so a read is an O(1) lookup), go hybrid by not pushing high-fan-out accounts but pulling-and-merging their recent posts at read time, rank a bounded candidate set by affinity/recency/predicted-engagement, hydrate IDs into content from a single post store at read time, run fan-out as an async queue/worker pipeline with at-least-once delivery and idempotent feed writes, cache active feeds and hot posts, and serve under eventual consistency with an always-render read path."

  revisitWhen="The follower distribution or read/write ratio shifts enough to retune the hot-user threshold; ranking becomes a primary differentiator needing a heavier real-time model; the feed must be globally distributed with regional freshness; or new surfaces (explore/recommendations from accounts you don't follow) change the candidate-generation problem from 'followees' to 'retrieval'."
/>

Four axes capture the real tension:

- **Push vs pull (write cost vs read cost).** Push precomputes feeds for O(1) reads at the cost of write amplification; pull is cheap to store and always fresh but pays a scatter-gather per read. Reads dominate, so push is the default — hybrid for the celebrity tail.
- **Chronological vs ranked.** Chronological is simple and predictable; ranked drives engagement but adds a scoring stage, model infrastructure, and filter-bubble concerns. Ranked wins for engagement, with chronological as the fallback.
- **Store IDs vs denormalized content.** IDs keep feeds tiny and make edits one write (hydrate at read); denormalized content makes reads need no hydration but turns every edit into millions of rewrites. IDs win.
- **Strong vs eventual freshness.** Strong consistency would make a post appear everywhere instantly but is costly and unnecessary; eventual (a few seconds) is the natural fit for a timeline, with the author's own post injected immediately for perceived freshness.
